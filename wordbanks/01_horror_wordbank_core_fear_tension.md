# Wordbank — Core Fear & Tension

## Metadata
- Category: Core Fear & Tension
- Purpose: Vocabulary for emotional escalation and threat perception
- Use Case: Short stories, narration scripts, psychological horror
- Tags: horror, fear, tension, emotion

---

## How to Use
- Select one precise emotion word per beat (avoid stacking synonyms)
- Anchor emotion to a concrete trigger (sound, movement, change)
- Escalate across scenes: suspicion → unease → anxiety → apprehension → dread → fear → alarm → panic → terror → desperation → helplessness → doom/hopelessness

---

## Escalation Ladder
Suspicion → Unease → Anxiety → Apprehension → Foreboding → Dread → Fear → Alarm → Panic → Terror → Desperation → Helplessness → Doom → Hopelessness

---

# Entries

## Suspicion
**Definition:** Sense that something is wrong or hidden  
**Context:** Early irregularities  
**Example:** A quiet suspicion formed when the photos looked different  
**Usage Tip:** Use when patterns begin to emerge  
**Why It Works:** Engages curiosity without confirming threat  

---

## Unease
**Definition:** Mild discomfort or disturbance  
**Context:** First tonal shift  
**Example:** Unease crept in as the room went silent  
**Usage Tip:** Open scenes with this  
**Why It Works:** Subtle tension keeps reader attentive  

---

## Anxiety
**Definition:** Worry about an uncertain outcome  
**Context:** Lingering discomfort  
**Example:** Anxiety lingered after the call ended  
**Usage Tip:** Use in slow scenes  
**Why It Works:** Feels realistic and relatable  

---

## Apprehension
**Definition:** Expectation that something bad will happen  
**Context:** Approaching danger  
**Example:** He reached for the door with apprehension  
**Usage Tip:** Use before entering unknown spaces  
**Why It Works:** Builds anticipation  

---

## Foreboding
**Definition:** Strong sense of impending danger  
**Context:** Environmental tone  
**Example:** The street carried a sense of foreboding  
**Usage Tip:** Use in scene setting  
**Why It Works:** Colors environment with threat  

---

## Dread
**Definition:** Fear of something inevitable  
**Context:** Pre-reveal tension  
**Example:** Dread settled in as footsteps stopped outside  
**Usage Tip:** Place before escalation  
**Why It Works:** Signals inevitability  

---

## Fear
**Definition:** Response to perceived danger  
**Context:** Baseline emotional state  
**Example:** Fear rose as the lights flickered  
**Usage Tip:** Pair with sensory triggers  
**Why It Works:** Bridges subtle and intense states  

---

## Alarm
**Definition:** Sudden awareness of danger  
**Context:** Immediate recognition  
**Example:** Alarm shot through him at the sound  
**Usage Tip:** Use for quick spikes  
**Why It Works:** Accelerates pacing  

---

## Panic
**Definition:** Uncontrolled fear disrupting thinking  
**Context:** Survival response  
**Example:** Panic set in when the door wouldn’t open  
**Usage Tip:** Show through actions  
**Why It Works:** Adds urgency and chaos  

---

## Terror
**Definition:** Extreme, overwhelming fear  
**Context:** Peak confrontation  
**Example:** Terror hit when it moved in the mirror  
**Usage Tip:** Reserve for climax  
**Why It Works:** Maximum emotional intensity  

---

## Desperation
**Definition:** Hopeless urgency  
**Context:** No good options remain  
**Example:** In desperation, he hid under the bed  
**Usage Tip:** Use late in scenes  
**Why It Works:** Signals instinct over logic  

---

## Helplessness
**Definition:** Inability to act or defend  
**Context:** Loss of control  
**Example:** He lay there, helpless as it approached  
**Usage Tip:** Pair with failed actions  
**Why It Works:** Removes agency  

---

## Vulnerability
**Definition:** Exposure to harm  
**Context:** Pre-threat setup  
**Example:** Alone in the dark, she felt vulnerable  
**Usage Tip:** Use before escalation  
**Why It Works:** Lowers safety  

---

## Paranoia
**Definition:** Irrational suspicion  
**Context:** Psychological instability  
**Example:** His paranoia grew each night  
**Usage Tip:** Use for unreliable perception  
**Why It Works:** Blurs internal vs external threat  

---

## Hysteria
**Definition:** Uncontrolled emotional breakdown  
**Context:** Extreme instability  
**Example:** Her voice rose into hysteria  
**Usage Tip:** Use sparingly  
**Why It Works:** Shows total loss of composure  

---

## Petrified
**Definition:** Too frightened to move  
**Context:** Freeze response  
**Example:** He stood petrified as it approached  
**Usage Tip:** Use in confrontation  
**Why It Works:** Highlights involuntary fear  

---

## Distress
**Definition:** Extreme anxiety or pain  
**Context:** Emotional + physical overlap  
**Example:** Distress filled his voice  
**Usage Tip:** Use in heavy scenes  
**Why It Works:** Deepens emotional impact  

---

## Agitation
**Definition:** Restless unease  
**Context:** Pre-panic buildup  
**Example:** His agitation grew in the silence  
**Usage Tip:** Show via movement  
**Why It Works:** Visible tension  

---

## Revulsion
**Definition:** Strong disgust  
**Context:** Body horror or decay  
**Example:** Revulsion turned her stomach  
**Usage Tip:** Pair with sensory detail  
**Why It Works:** Combines fear and disgust  

---

## Awe
**Definition:** Wonder mixed with fear  
**Context:** Cosmic or large-scale horror  
**Example:** He stared upward in awe  
**Usage Tip:** Use for scale  
**Why It Works:** Expands threat beyond comprehension  

---

## Doom
**Definition:** Inevitable destruction  
**Context:** Late-stage narrative  
**Example:** A sense of doom filled the house  
**Usage Tip:** Use near ending  
**Why It Works:** Removes hope  

---

## Hopelessness
**Definition:** Belief that nothing will improve  
**Context:** Final emotional state  
**Example:** Hopelessness settled in as the path looped  
**Usage Tip:** Use after repeated failure  
**Why It Works:** Leaves lasting impact  

---

# Usage Patterns

## Emotion + Trigger
Panic hit when the lights went out  
→ Anchors emotion to action  

---

## Emotion + Environment
Dread filled the hallway  
→ Turns setting into threat  

---

## Emotion + Realization
That’s when the fear became real  
→ Marks transition  

---

## Avoid Word Stacking
Weak: fear, panic, terror  
Strong: Panic set in when the door locked  

---

# Scene Timing

Early:
- suspicion
- unease
- anxiety  

Mid:
- apprehension
- dread
- alarm  

Climax:
- panic
- terror
- hysteria  

Ending:
- doom
- hopelessness
- helplessness  

---

# Key Principles

- Use precise words, not many  
- Escalate gradually  
- Anchor emotion to physical triggers  
- Match word to pacing  
- Use strong words sparingly

---

## Integration Rule

Emotion selection MUST align with:

- escalation stage from `/maps/escalation_map.md`
- scene progression from `/maps/scene_flow_map.md`

Do NOT:

- use high-intensity emotions in early stages
- skip emotional progression
- apply emotions that do not match pacing

Emotion must follow escalation, not lead it.
