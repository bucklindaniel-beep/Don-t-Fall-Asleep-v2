# Horror Word Bank — Physical Sensation & Body

## Metadata
- Category: Physical Sensation & Body
- Purpose: Vocabulary for describing bodily reactions, physical tension, and visceral horror
- Use Case: Body horror, survival horror, cinematic narration, high-immersion scenes
- Tags: horror, body, sensation, physical, visceral, movement, injury, writing

---

## How to Use This File
This word bank is designed to:
- Ground horror in physical experience
- Translate fear into bodily reactions
- Increase immersion through sensory detail
- Support action without losing tension

Each entry includes:
- Definition (standard meaning)
- Context (how it functions in horror)
- Example (usable sentence)
- Usage Tip (practical application)
- Why It Works (psychological or narrative reasoning)

---

## Core Principle
Fear becomes real when the body reacts.

The body should:
- Respond before the mind understands
- Reveal danger through involuntary reactions
- Carry tension through sensation, not explanation

---

## Physical Escalation Model (Extractable Framework)

Subtle Sensation → Tension → Reaction → Loss of Control → Contact → Damage → Aftermath

---

# Vocabulary Entries

## Tension
**Definition:** Mental or emotional strain; physical tightness  

**Context:**  
Used as the earliest bodily response to stress  

**Example:**  
Tension built in his shoulders as the noise continued  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use before visible action begins  

**Why It Works:**  
Signals the body reacting before conscious understanding  

---

## Tightness
**Definition:** A feeling of constriction or pressure  

**Context:**  
Used in chest, throat, muscles  

**Example:**  
A tightness formed in his chest  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use to indicate suppressed panic  

**Why It Works:**  
Suggests buildup without release  

---

## Tremor
**Definition:** Involuntary shaking movement  

**Context:**  
Used in hands, voice, or body  

**Example:**  
A slight tremor ran through his hands  

**Usage Tip:**  
Keep small at first for realism  

**Why It Works:**  
Shows fear physically without exaggeration  

---

## Shaking

**Context:**  
Used during fear escalation  

**Example:**

**Usage Tip:**  
Use when control is slipping  

**Why It Works:**  
Indicates loss of composure  

---

## Pulse

# Usage Patterns

## Sensation Before Explanation
Example:
Something cold touched his arm  

Why:
Forces reader to feel before understanding  

---

## Reaction Before Cause
Example:
He flinched—then heard it  

Why:
Reverses expectation  

---

## Escalation Through Contact
Example:
First a brush. Then pressure. Then a grip  

Why:
Builds physical progression  

---

## Loss of Control Sequence
Example:
His hands shook. Then stopped responding  

Why:
Shows progression into helplessness

# Scene Construction Reference (Extractable)

1. Introduce subtle sensation  
2. Build tension in body  
3. Trigger involuntary reaction  
4. Escalate to movement or restriction  
5. Introduce contact  
6. Apply force or damage  
7. Extend aftermath  

---

# Key Principles

- The body reacts before the mind understands  
- Use small sensations before large ones  
- Anchor fear in physical experience  
- Avoid over-description of gore  
- Let contact be rare and meaningful

## Integration Rule

Physical sensation MUST align with:

- escalation stage from `/maps/escalation_map.md`
- scene progression from `/maps/scene_flow_map.md`
- entity behavior from `/analysis/entities/`

You MUST:

- progress from subtle sensation → involuntary reaction → loss of control → contact → damage
- delay physical contact as long as possible
- increase intensity gradually

You MUST NOT:

- introduce physical damage too early
- rely on gore instead of sensation
- separate physical reaction from psychological or environmental cues

The body must reflect the presence of the threat before the threat is fully revealed.
