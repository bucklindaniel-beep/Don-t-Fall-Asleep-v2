# Horror Word Bank — Psychological Horror

## Metadata
- Category: Psychological
- Purpose: Vocabulary for internal fear, perception distortion, and mental instability
- Use Case: Psychological horror, analog horror, unreliable narrator, high-retention narration
- Tags: horror, psychological, paranoia, perception, mind, tension, writing

---

## How to Use This File
This word bank is designed to:
- Blur the line between reality and perception
- Create internal conflict and uncertainty
- Sustain tension without visible threats
- Increase immersion through implication and ambiguity

Each entry includes:
- Definition (standard meaning)
- Context (how it functions in horror)
- Example (usable sentence)
- Usage Tip (practical application)
- Why It Works (psychological or narrative reasoning)

---

## Core Principle
Psychological horror is not about what is seen.

It is about:
- What might be there
- What cannot be confirmed
- What the character begins to believe

---

## Psychological Escalation Model (Extractable Framework)

Stability → Unease → Doubt → Suspicion → Paranoia → Distortion → Loss of Control → Breakdown

Use this progression to:
- Gradually destabilize the character’s perception
- Maintain ambiguity as long as possible
- Delay confirmation of threat

# Vocabulary Entries

## Doubt
**Definition:** A feeling of uncertainty or lack of conviction  

**Context:**  
Used when the character questions their own perception  

**Example:**  
For a moment, he doubted what he saw  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use immediately after something unusual occurs  

**Why It Works:**  
Introduces instability without confirming anything  

---

## Suspicion
**Definition:** A belief that something is wrong or hidden  

**Context:**  
Used when patterns begin to emerge  

**Example:**  
Suspicion crept in as the same noise repeated  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use when the character starts connecting events  

**Why It Works:**  
Transforms passive fear into active awareness  

---

## Paranoia
**Definition:** Irrational or exaggerated distrust or fear  

**Context:**  
Used when the character begins assuming threat everywhere  

**Example:**  
His paranoia grew with every passing hour  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use when the character over-interprets normal stimuli  

**Why It Works:**  
Blurs internal vs external threat  

---

## Uneasy
**Definition:** Causing discomfort or anxiety  

**Context:**  
Used to subtly shift tone  

**Example:**  
Something about the room felt uneasy  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use in early-stage perception shifts  

**Why It Works:**  
Creates discomfort without requiring explanation  

---

## Unsettling
**Definition:** Causing anxiety or disturbance  

**Context:**  
Used for subtle wrongness  

**Example:**  
There was something unsettling about the silence  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use for environmental or behavioral irregularities  

**Why It Works:**  
Signals that something is off without defining it  

---

## Watching
**Definition:** Observing closely  

**Context:**  
Used when the character feels observed  

**Example:**  
He felt like something was watching him  

**Usage Tip:**  
Do not confirm what is watching  

**Why It Works:**  
Triggers primal fear of being prey  

---

## Presence
**Definition:** The feeling that something is nearby  

**Context:**  
Used without visual confirmation  

**Example:**  
There was a presence behind him  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use instead of naming a threat  

**Why It Works:**  
Creates tension through absence of clarity  

---

## Awareness
**Definition:** Knowledge or perception of a situation  

**Context:**  
Used when the character becomes conscious of something  

**Example:**  
A sudden awareness came over him  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use as a transition into fear  

**Why It Works:**  
Marks a mental shift rather than physical action  

---

## Distorted
**Definition:** Twisted or altered from normal  

**Context:**  
Used for perception errors or visual inconsistencies  

**Example:**  
Her reflection appeared slightly distorted  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use in mirrors, voices, or familiar objects  

**Why It Works:**  
Breaks trust in reality  

---

## Warped
**Definition:** Bent or twisted out of shape  

**Context:**  
Used for reality or memory distortion  

**Example:**  
The hallway felt warped  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use when space or logic changes subtly  

**Why It Works:**  
Suggests reality is unreliable  

---

## Wrong
**Definition:** Not correct or natural  

**Context:**  
Used for subtle but undeniable irregularities  

**Example:**  
Everything looked normal, but something was wrong  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use in simple sentences for impact  

**Why It Works:**  
Relies on intuition rather than explanation  

---

## Off
**Definition:** Slightly incorrect or unusual  

**Context:**  
Used for minimal but noticeable differences  

**Example:**  
Something felt off about his voice  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use when change is small but important  

**Why It Works:**  
Creates discomfort through subtle deviation  

---

## Familiar
**Definition:** Recognizable or known  

**Context:**  
Used when something should feel safe  

**Example:**  
The house looked familiar  

**Usage Tip:**  
Pair with contrast (familiar but wrong)  

**Why It Works:**  
Corrupts safety into discomfort  

---

## Unfamiliar
**Definition:** Not known or recognized  

**Context:**  
Used when expected things change  

**Example:**  
The room felt unfamiliar  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use after a known environment shifts  

**Why It Works:**  
Destroys sense of control  

---

## Memory
**Definition:** The faculty of recalling past events  

**Context:**  
Used when past and present conflict  

**Example:**  
His memory didn’t match what he was seeing  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use to create contradictions  

**Why It Works:**  
Undermines trust in reality  

---

## Hallucination
**Definition:** Perception without external stimulus  

**Context:**  
Used when reality is uncertain  

**Example:**  
He wondered if it was a hallucination  

**Usage Tip:**  
Do not confirm if it is real  

**Why It Works:**  
Maintains ambiguity  

---

## Delusion
**Definition:** A false belief held despite evidence  

**Context:**  
Used in mental breakdown  

**Example:**  
The delusion became harder to ignore  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use late in escalation  

**Why It Works:**  
Shows loss of rational thought  

---

## Obsession
**Definition:** Persistent, intrusive thought  

**Context:**  
Used when the character fixates  

**Example:**  
The sound became an obsession  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use for repeated elements  

**Why It Works:**  
Reinforces psychological loops  

---

## Intrusion
**Definition:** Unwanted entry or interruption  

**Context:**  
Used for thoughts, sounds, or presence  

**Example:**  
The thought felt like an intrusion  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use when something violates mental space  

**Why It Works:**  
Creates discomfort beyond physical reality  

---

## Breakdown
**Definition:** Loss of mental stability  

**Context:**  
Used at peak psychological collapse  

**Example:**  
He felt himself breaking down  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use near climax  

**Why It Works:**  
Signals total loss of control  

---

## Dissociation
**Definition:** Disconnection from reality or self  

**Context:**  
Used when character feels detached  

**Example:**  
Everything felt distant, unreal  

**Usage Tip:**  
Use during extreme stress  

**Why It Works:**  
Creates surreal, dreamlike horror

# Usage Patterns

## Perception vs Reality
Example:
He saw it move—but wasn’t sure it had  

Why:
Maintains ambiguity  

---

## Familiar → Wrong
Example:
The room was the same. Almost  

Why:
Corrupts safety  

---

## Thought Intrusion
Example:
The idea came suddenly—and wouldn’t leave  

Why:
Creates internal conflict  

---

## Repetition Loop
Example:
The sound came again. And again. And again  

Why:
Builds psychological pressure  

---

## Delayed Confirmation
Example:
He waited for it to happen again  

Why:
Extends tension

1. Environment introduces unease  
2. Character notices irregularity  
3. Doubt forms  
4. Suspicion builds  
5. Perception becomes unreliable  
6. Paranoia escalates  
7. Reality distorts  
8. Breakdown or reveal

# Key Principles

- Never confirm too early  
- Let the character question reality  
- Use repetition to build pressure  
- Favor implication over explanation  
- Keep threats partially undefined

## Integration Rule

Psychological language MUST align with:

- escalation stage from `/maps/escalation_flow_map.md`
- scene progression from `/maps/scene_flow_map.md`
- entity behavior from `/analysis/entities/`

You MUST:

- shift from doubt → paranoia → distortion → breakdown progressively

You MUST NOT:

- introduce paranoia or breakdown in early stages
- resolve ambiguity too early
- separate psychological effects from entity influence

Psychological horror must evolve alongside the threat, not independently.
