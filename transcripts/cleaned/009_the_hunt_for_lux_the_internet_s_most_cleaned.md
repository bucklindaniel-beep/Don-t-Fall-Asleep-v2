## Story 01 — The Hunt for Lux

In 2011, Anonymous initiated Operation Darknet, launching coordinated attacks against abuse websites, publicly shaming their users. The operation was praised publicly, but had an unintended consequence: it advertised the existence of these sites to new individuals. One of those individuals, known only as Lux, downloaded the Tor browser and entered the dark web for the first time.

Lux became drawn to harmful content he found there, and over time he grew desensitized. He described himself as becoming increasingly comfortable with his interests. After immersing himself in these communities, he identified two problems with the existing sites: poor security and a lack of extreme content.

He offered his technical skills to improve security across multiple sites, which quickly raised his status in these communities. His skills became sought after, particularly as Operation Darknet had heightened concerns about anonymity. He went on to work across many of the top sites and educated other users on practices such as stripping metadata from uploaded files.

After gaining further influence, Lux turned his attention to creating his own content. In 2012, he published multiple sites of his own creation on the dark web. These sites hosted what became known as hurtcore content — a term combining hurt and hardcore. All of his sites featured illegal content involving minors. He quickly became known as the most prominent source of this material, drawing hundreds of users.

By 2013, law enforcement began responding to the increased traffic on the dark web following several high-profile operations. The FBI seized and shut down Freedom Hosting, the largest server of this type of material. Lux was using this service. With its shutdown, all of his sites were taken offline. The FBI had also planted malware that exposed dozens of users during this takedown.

Lux was not identified by this malware. After a brief period, he reestablished his sites by investing in his own personal hosting service and migrating his operations. He publicly taunted law enforcement, posting messages declaring that the empire had not fallen and that every attempt to shut him down would only make the community larger.

Following this, Lux expanded further. He declared his portfolio of sites an empire, referring to it as the Pedo Empire. This included his most well-known site, Hurt to the Core, and several others. He spent significant time each night optimizing these sites, even while acknowledging to himself that consequences were likely inevitable.

Law enforcement intensified its efforts. Lux was added to the FBI's most wanted list for offenders of this type. Investigators found conflicting information about his identity. His activity timestamps suggested a Southern Hemisphere location, but his own posts claimed he was a fifty-year-old pediatrician from the United States who described having regular access to children.

Lux never appeared visually on any of his sites. Law enforcement used multiple tactics to identify him but described the effort as searching for a ghost.

As his empire grew, Lux built additional properties. One of these was structured like a wiki dedicated to his communities, containing approximately 600 articles and attracting over 1,000 contributors. The wiki included instructional material and received over three million visits.

His flagship site, Hurt to the Core, operated with a tiered access structure. New users were limited to surface-level content. To access higher tiers, users were required to submit their own original material, never previously seen online. The highest-tier areas were called producer lounges and required verified original production. Evidence found on the site suggested that some users exploited professional roles that gave them access to vulnerable individuals.

Because of this access model, law enforcement agents posing as users were quickly identified and removed as leeches. They could not submit the required content and therefore never gained access to higher tiers. This made the site extremely difficult to infiltrate.

Lux boasted that his sites received 400,000 hits per day and that Hurt to the Core alone had 15,000 daily video downloads. He described himself as having complete control over the largest network of verified producers on any platform of this type. He moderated all communities personally and banned users for rule violations.

Lux made contact with other notable individuals in related communities. He became interested in a video known as Daisy's Destruction, considered one of the most extreme pieces of this type of content ever produced, made by an individual named Peter Scully. Lux attempted to obtain a copy, initially offering an amount that was immediately rejected. He continued negotiating through other channels and eventually obtained partial footage from an unknown source.

He released this footage on his site, both as revenge against the original seller and to further his status. The release drew significant attention and elevated his reputation within the community. Among wider dark web communities, however, his name became synonymous with cruelty. Even among others engaged in related illegal activity, many drew a line at the level of content Lux was hosting. Lux began receiving approximately a dozen death threats per week.

In response to criticism, Lux opened a secondary site called Love to the Core, described as a softer version of his empire. Despite this, his reputation kept many potential users away, with some publicly stating they did not want to be associated with someone responsible for distributing the Daisy's Destruction footage.

Law enforcement continued to pursue him. Several of his associates were identified and arrested, but none were able to identify Lux himself. In 2014, investigators received an anonymous tip suggesting Lux might be located in Melbourne, Australia. This contradicted his claims of being a United States doctor, but was consistent with his active hours. A joint investigation involving the FBI, Interpol, and the Victorian police launched a full-scale international manhunt.

Around this time, Lux sensed that law enforcement was closing in. He sent an email to the FBI's cybercrime tip line offering to hand over his entire empire, including his knowledge of thousands of users' identities, in exchange for fifty thousand dollars in Bitcoin and full immunity in all jurisdictions. The FBI declined. Lux followed up with additional messages urging them to reconsider. They did not.

On June 24th, 2014, Lux announced he was shutting down the Pedo Empire, citing personal issues. Members of his communities reacted with posts in his honor. Lux retreated to secondary accounts to avoid detection.

Law enforcement had by now narrowed their suspect list to one individual. Based on the anonymous tip and follow-up investigation, they identified a home in Melbourne, Australia, belonging to a working-class mechanic in his fifties. When officers searched the property, however, it became clear the older man had no knowledge of computers. Attention shifted to his son, a twenty-one-year-old who rarely left his room, spent extensive time gaming online, and had no presence on any social media platform.

Officers lacked enough evidence to make a formal arrest or seize equipment. They asked the son to unlock his devices, and he refused. Then, voluntarily, he offered to show police his phone, evidently confident it contained nothing. On the surface it was clean, but in the phone's cache, investigators found three images of illegal material. This was sufficient to arrest Matthew Graham.

Investigators still could not break the encryption on his computer. With only three images as evidence, they could not hold him indefinitely. They sent one hard drive to Europol in Germany and another to the FBI in Washington DC. The records of what was uncovered are partly redacted, but the FBI was eventually able to establish irrefutable proof that Matthew Graham was Lux.

An investigator visited him in Australian custody and offered an ultimatum: face charges related to running the empire in the United States, or remain in Australia and admit to everything. Matthew chose to stay in Australia and provided his sixty-four-character password.

With access to his drives confirmed, investigators found substantial illegal material and evidence across all charges. Matthew pleaded guilty to thirteen counts.

During trial, further details emerged. Matthew described himself as a strong believer in free speech, using this as justification for his behavior. He claimed to be asexual and said he had no interest in children, despite having previously admitted to a journalist that he was attracted to ten-year-olds. His parents, sitting in court, were unaware that he had built this operation under their roof, believing he had simply been gaming. Graffiti found in his bedroom closet read: Parents should be afraid of raising children like us.

Among the charges was aiding and abetting crimes in Russia, in which Matthew advised an abuser on how to carry out and record a murder, with the promise of connecting him to buyers. Another charge involved a disabled, mute seven-year-old in the UK, with Matthew commenting on how her inability to speak worked in the abuser's favor.

It was also disclosed that during the time he was running the empire, Matthew worked as a babysitter for six children. No evidence was found that he harmed any of them.

The judge was required to view the Daisy's Destruction footage in order to understand the scope of the case. He viewed it privately, returned visibly pale, and described Matthew's case as without parallel. Several people in the courtroom left during the description of the material alone.

The judge described the crimes as pure evil. Matthew received a sentence of fifteen years, with the possibility of parole after ten. Having already served five hundred and seventy days at time of sentencing in 2016, Lux became eligible for parole during the recording of this video. His sentence expires in 2029 at the latest.

The sentence drew public condemnation from around the world. Critics noted that if he had been extradited to the United States, the sentence would likely have been significantly longer.

As a partial outcome of the investigation, multiple other offenders were identified and arrested using data recovered from Lux's empire. Thirty young victims were identified and rescued as a result. Australia also enacted a new law making it a criminal offense to withhold a password to an encrypted device when served with a lawful order to do so.
