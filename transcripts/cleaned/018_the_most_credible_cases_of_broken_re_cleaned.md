## Story 01 — The 18 Ponds of the Dragon King (Fay, China)

Early on the morning of February 16th, 2020, a 39-year-old woman named Fay stepped out of a convenience store in a small village in China. She was wearing a bright red distinctive coat, and in her hands were a whole bunch of snacks she had just purchased.

Fay had lived in this village her whole life. She was a very simple person and she had a really specific daily schedule that she basically always adhered to. She would get up early, throw on her jacket, head out to the convenience store, get some snacks, and then either go back home where she lived with her husband, or she would go to her parents' house or her sister's house to pay them a visit. That was basically what she did every single day.

This day had started exactly the same as every other day. After she left the store and began eating some snacks, thinking about where she was going to go next, she was walking down this road when suddenly she heard people calling out to her. She stopped and turned. There were five people clustered together standing at the top of a trailhead.

Fay's village was home to this beautiful hiking trail. It started right where these people were standing, went up the mountainside and into a huge forest where there were ponds and waterfalls. The forest was called the 18 Ponds of the Dragon King because there were literally 18 ponds scattered throughout it. The village where Fay lived also meant "Dragon King" in Chinese.

Even though Fay had grown up here, she had never once gone on that hiking trail. There was a reason for that. When she was younger, she had a tumor, and to get rid of it she had to take medication that left her with serious long-term effects. Even though the tumor went away and she recovered, the process instilled in her this mentality that she was frail and delicate and shouldn't take any chances. She really never did anything that was even remotely risky — like hiking up into the mountains. So instead, she developed a really simple, safe daily routine of getting snacks and visiting family. While in some ways this kept Fay safe, it also made her very isolated and lonely.

So Fay is on this road, and these five villagers at the trailhead have just deliberately called to her by name. They're saying, "Hey, Fay, over here." They're clearly getting ready to go hike up into the 18 Ponds of the Dragon King, and they tell her, "Come with us. Come hiking with us. Come check it out."

For Fay, this was so unusual. Something in her felt really excited. Why not do something different for once? How dangerous could it be? She was with other people. They seemed competent. So Fay drifted over to these five friendly people and said, "What the heck? Yeah, I'll come with you. I've never been up there. Let's go."

Over the next couple of hours, Fay was seeing a beautiful landscape that she had been so close to her whole life and was now seeing for the first time. It was revelatory. Her mood was so good. The five people were wonderful — they all chattered, talked about their lives, and Fay opened up about hers and her health scares.

Eventually they made their way into the dense forest and reached one of the ponds. It had a huge clearing, sunlight pouring in, and a waterfall feeding into the pond. Stunningly beautiful. Fay's friends took a seat on the shore and broke out some snacks. Fay sat down with the snacks she'd bought from the convenience store, and they basically had a picnic on the shore.

For Fay, it was the most amazing day potentially of her whole life. She had broken out of her comfort zone, met these wonderful, inclusive people, and was experiencing unbelievable natural beauty.

But as she was sitting there, she started to feel really intensely fatigued. At first she thought, "Of course — I don't really get out much, I just hiked up this mountain." She felt herself slumping back on the rock she was on, almost positioning herself to lie down. The world around her began getting hazy. Her friends were still talking, but they weren't really looking at her — just enjoying their food. Fay couldn't keep her eyes open. She put her head down, closed her eyes, and fell asleep.

When Fay woke up again, she was still in that same spot — but now it was dark, and her five friends were gone. She was alone next to the pond in the middle of the forest.

A jolt of panic rocketed through her. She looked around for her friends. They had to be here somewhere. They wouldn't just abandon her. But all she could see was darkness. There was some moonlight coming through the clearing over the pond, but the treeline was all dark, and there was no one. All she could hear were birds and night insects.

Her mind started telling her: this whole thing was a trap. Those five people weren't her friends. They had brought her up here, tricked her, drugged her — or maybe they would come back and hurt her.

She turned and looked at the trail that had brought them up. It was just a dark treeline, a cut path through the trees that was basically pitch black. She didn't have a light to guide her. But she stood up, felt very weak on her feet but powered by adrenaline, and started stumbling into the treeline. She kept deviating from the path because it was so dark, kept bumping into trees, kept hearing noises all around her — convinced they were the five people coming back, or animals, or something.

For hours she stumbled through, completely disoriented. Eventually she came to a stream. She knelt down and scooped water into her mouth. Right as the sun came up across the mountainside, there was enough light that she could miraculously see a path down the side of the mountain that she believed would bring her back to her village.

She caught her breath, took another sip from the stream, and with the help of the sunlight, navigated to the trail. Down the mountain she went. As she descended, she looked toward her village and saw clearly: there was a big group of people, presumably villagers, out looking for her — right down at the start of the trail.

Fay waved and called out. A few people in the crowd looked up at her. There was some level of recognition, but she couldn't really tell what their reaction was. She got their attention. Suddenly she felt rejuvenated — she was going to be okay.

She got closer to the crowd. It was friends. Family. Definitely her people. But the looks on their faces were not what Fay was expecting. They were looks of shock and horror. Not relief.

When Fay got close enough to make out individual faces, she saw her sister and waved. "Hey, I'm okay. It's okay. Everything's fine. Come on."

Her sister broke ranks very hesitantly, jogged up to Fay, and the two embraced. Her sister said, "Where have you been?"

Fay stepped back and said, "Oh, you know, yesterday — or a few hours ago, I don't know, I kind of lost track of time. I saw these five people, we made our way into the mountains. I shouldn't have done it, but I fell asleep and here I am. I'm so sorry if I scared you, but I'm okay."

Her sister was hearing what Fay was saying but couldn't process it. Eventually she stepped back, looked at Fay, and just said, "That can't be true. You've been gone for 17 days."

Fay didn't really have time to appreciate what she had just been told. She was rushed to the hospital, where doctors discovered she had lost 60 pounds. She was horribly malnourished and dehydrated. They didn't understand how she was still alive.

While Fay was being treated, she began to hear what had happened over the past 17 days. Security footage showed when she went into the woods — they caught her walking into the forest in her bright red distinctive coat. They knew she was up in the 7½-mile stretch of the 18 Ponds of the Dragon King. Because of strict COVID-era checkpoints between villages, they knew Fay had not gone into any surrounding villages. She had to be in that 7½-mile stretch.

A huge search party went into the area. They fired off fireworks left and right to try to get her attention. Nobody found any trace of Fay. And from her perspective, she said, "I never heard fireworks. I never heard or saw any searchers." It literally felt like she had fallen asleep for a few hours and woken up — not 17 days later.

Eventually Fay went into detail about the specifics of what she remembered: those five people, her new friends. She remembered their names, what they looked like, the discussions she'd had with them. She painted them as wonderful people, but said it was like she just woke up and they were gone. She thought maybe they had drugged her and that's why she couldn't remember more.

After Fay said that, her family looked at each other. "Fay, those can't be the five people you were with."

"Why?"

"Because the five people you named and described — they've been dead for a long time now."

Fay was incredulous. But when they reviewed the security footage that showed Fay clearly in her bright red distinctive coat walking up the trail into the 18 Ponds, there was nobody else with her. Those five people weren't on camera.

The 18 Ponds of the Dragon King is not just a beautiful hiking destination. It's also believed to be haunted. Locals claim that when they look up at the mountainside they can sometimes see eerie blue lights moving around in the trees that nobody can explain. There's a story that people who have died, their spirits sort of haunt the area looking for living people to switch places with.

When Fay began coming down the mountainside and she looked and saw that huge crowd of people she believed was her search party — well, that wasn't actually the case. One of the locals had looked up and seen what he thought was another one of those lights. Not blue, but red. It was Fay's red jacket. He had seen this red thing bouncing around in the distance and thought it was one of these strange lights — a ghost. He told people in the village. Everybody ran out to go see what it was. It sort of was a ghost. It was Fay, who'd been gone for 17 days, coming down the mountain — and nobody could believe it.

To this day, nobody knows what actually happened to Fay. All anyone knows is they have her on camera disappearing into the trail, into the forest, into the mountainside — and then turning back up 17 days later. Before this, the longest anybody had survived out there after getting lost was 12 days, and that was a young man who was really fit and barely survived. Fay was in horrible shape and she was out there for 17 days. Somehow she showed up alive.

Fay still lives in the village today. Her story, at least from her perspective, has never changed.

## Story 02 — Lin Wong-Yao / Zu Shua-Wa Reincarnation Case (Taiwan, 1959–1962)

One morning in the spring of 1959, a middle-aged civil engineer named Wu Chiu biked through his rural town in Taiwan. He was on his way home from work. Normally he enjoyed this bike ride home — taking his time looking at the beautiful temple and the marketplaces. But today he was in a huge rush because he had gotten word that his wife had become horribly sick.

Wu was pedaling as fast as he possibly could. As he got to his neighborhood, he started to realize he was really gassed. Pedaling to his house was unbelievably exhausting. He was an athletic guy and this type of ride was something he should have been able to handle. It seemed like there was a weight attached to him, like he was pulling an extra 100 pounds.

He pushed through the pain. He turned onto his street, and as he was making his final approach, he saw one of his neighbors. Even though Wu was completely in a rush, he was polite. Sweating and hustling, he turned and waved.

His neighbor clearly looked at him, made eye contact, saw it was Wu — and instead of waving back, scowled at him. Like they were disgusted by him. They turned and went the other way.

Wu thought, "What was that about?" But he had to get home, so he wrote it off and kept pedaling.

Closer to his house, another neighbor came outside. Wu, sweating profusely, said, "Hey, how are you?" This neighbor also looked at Wu with total disdain. Wu thought, "What did I do?" That neighbor went back inside their house.

Wu finally got to his house, parked his bike, and ran inside.

He found his wife in the bedroom in bed, pale and sick. Her name was Lin Wong-Yao. She was 37 years old, and generally a pretty frail person, prone to minor illnesses. Wu was accustomed to her not doing well. But as soon as he saw her, he could tell whatever was going on was serious. This was not one of her typical bouts.

Wu began to tend to her. He made her soup and tea, but Lin barely could sip either. She had a fever that just kept getting worse. Wu was up tending to her all night, hoping she would break the fever. She didn't.

This was 1959, in a rural village in Taiwan. It was difficult to get to a hospital. Wu wanted to find a way to treat her at home and was praying she'd show signs of getting better. Over the next couple of days, Lin did not get better. Her fever never broke. She basically stopped eating and drinking, drifting in and out of consciousness.

After three days, Wu knew he had to get her to a hospital. The primary hurdle was transportation. He didn't have a car, but one of his neighbors had a donkey cart.

Wu went outside and found that neighbor with their cart. He flagged them down and asked if they'd be willing to transport Lin to the hospital. Just like the earlier neighbors, this one — who knew Wu well — looked at him with one of those looks of disgust and turned around like they weren't even going to talk to him.

Wu in desperation said, "Look, my wife — she's dying. She needs to get to the hospital. Please, do it for her."

The neighbor, despite their misgivings, turned back and said, "Fine."

They got Lin onto the cart. It took several hours, but they reached the hospital. By the time they arrived, Lin was basically unconscious. Doctors and nurses tried to stabilize her. Her condition only got worse. That night she slipped into a coma and died.

Wu was devastated. He and the neighbor took Lin's body back to their town. Wu began setting up for a funeral service. In Taiwan, it's customary during funerals for the body of the loved one to be brought into the house, where it stays for seven days before cremation.

Wu put Lin into a coffin and set her up in the living room. Over the next several days, friends and family — even the neighbors who had given Wu strange looks — came in to pay their respects. As Wu listened to people pray over the body and talk about how wonderful Lin was, the reality hit him. Lin was a simple woman — illiterate, didn't travel, introverted, quiet — but a wonderful wife. She took care of him, was a wonderful cook, made people around her feel safe and happy. He kept having fits of crying.

On the third day of the funeral service, Wu was in his living room dealing with overwhelming sadness, with people coming in and out. At some point, the room just suddenly went completely silent. People began to gasp.

Wu had been head down. He noticed the change in the room and looked up. A few of the mourners were open-mouthed and horrified, looking at the casket.

Wu looked over. There in the open casket was Lin — sitting upright, looking around the room, clearly alive.

Wu's first reaction was that Lin must have slipped into a really deep coma — so deep the doctors couldn't distinguish it from death. But at the same time, he was thinking: he had not left her side these three days. She hadn't moved, hadn't made a sound, certainly hadn't eaten or drunk anything. This didn't seem possible.

The mourners jumped into action, gently lifted Lin out of the casket, and carried her to her bed. Lin gestured for water. She was too weak to speak. She had lost a lot of weight, but she sipped water. Everyone looked at Wu like, "We have to get her to the hospital. What else can we do?"

Wu looked at his wife. She seemed too frail to survive another donkey cart ride. He decided they had to keep her there and hope she'd recover enough that maybe in a day or two they could bring her in.

Over the next few hours, Wu and friends and family stayed by Lin's bedside, constantly checking on her. She continued to drink water and have a little food. Amazingly, she went from basically being dead to seeming to get a whole lot better over the span of only a few hours. Lin was actually sitting up in bed, stretching, moving around as if completely fine. In Wu's opinion, she seemed almost physically better off than she was normally when alive.

By that night, when Lin really did seem back to her normal self, the mourners left. It was just Wu and Lin. Wu was grateful she was alive, even though none of it made sense.

The next morning, Lin was so much better that she was out of bed walking around the house, excited to face the day. Wu thought, "You were dead yesterday. What's going on?"

Lin said to him, "Hey, I want to go on a walk. Want to go with me? Go to the temple."

The two of them went outside and began to walk. Wu — athletic — found himself barely able to keep pace with Lin, who was speed-walking toward the temple. As they walked, neighbors would wave to them and chat briefly. Wu noticed something really strange as Lin spoke. Her accent was different. She didn't sound like his wife.

The weirdness around Lin's miraculous recovery only got more weird over time.

Before Lin died and came back, she had been illiterate. Now she was not only able to read, but she could read and speak fluent Mandarin — a language she didn't know before she died.

Lin used to be a really skilled cook who loved cooking meat dishes in particular. Now that Lin had recovered, she no longer wanted to eat meat. She said she was a vegetarian and basically stopped cooking meat.

Lin began speaking with real authority about accounting and bookkeeping. She advised her husband to quit his job as an engineer and open up a building materials shop in town because there was a real need for it and he'd make real money that way.

Wu didn't understand what was going on, but his wife really seemed like a force to be reckoned with now. However she had gained all this extra knowledge, she was sharp these days. He quit his engineering job, opened a building materials shop, and the two of them began making real money — more than they had ever made before.

Wu and Lin became the biggest curiosity in town. The story of Lin's miraculous return with the bizarre personality changes spread across the country. Eventually it reached the Taiwanese government, and they decided to investigate.

On March 17th, 1962, the Taiwanese government sent for Lin. Lin was very happy to go. She was ready to tell her story. They scooped her up and brought her to an army hospital, where she would be meeting with the chief physician.

The chief physician was inside a room waiting. He'd never met Lin before. He'd only been told roughly: there's a woman acting funny after coming back from the dead.

Lin walked in. Before he could begin a line of questioning, Lin looked at him with a double take — a moment of recognition — and said: "Uncle."

The chief physician was confused. "Why are you calling me uncle?"

Lin would explain why. The chief physician was so shocked he left the room and called for his wife. When his wife came to the hospital, he didn't tell her what was going on — he just said, "Come with me." He, his wife, and Lin — who'd never met this woman — were in the room together. Lin looked at her and just said: "Auntie."

Lin then revealed that her real name wasn't actually Lin. That's what people called her, but it wasn't who she was. Her real name was Zu Shua-Wa.

The chief physician and his wife couldn't believe it. They had not given any information about their personal lives to Lin. She had simply offered up what she claimed her name was, called them uncle and auntie. And as it turned out, the chief physician and his wife did have a niece whose name was Zu Shua-Wa. A year earlier, their niece had gone missing during the Chinese Civil War and had been presumed dead. Her body had never been found.

According to Lin, Zu had died during that war — robbed and murdered by a group of fishermen. After she was killed, her spirit was stuck in purgatory for a time. A year after Zu's death, when Lin died, Zu's spirit entered Lin's body. She came back to life — Zu did, in a different body. Lin was telling them she was Zu, reincarnated in Lin's body.

As crazy as this sounded, the chief physician and his wife couldn't help but notice that as Lin was speaking, her accent was exactly like Zu's. Lin was acting exactly like their niece.

When they dug deeper and spoke more to Lin and her husband Wu, they learned that, since the reincarnation:
- Lin was suddenly a vegetarian, just like Zu
- Lin was interested in accounting and bookkeeping — Zu's interests
- Lin had been illiterate but could now read and speak Mandarin fluently — the language Zu read and spoke

The chief physician started to believe that maybe Lin was telling the truth — maybe this really was his niece's reincarnated spirit. He told the Taiwanese government that something weird was happening here. To pressure-test it, he proposed: Lin claims she knows who killed his niece. She had given the names of the fishermen who allegedly robbed and killed Zu. They should investigate his niece's disappearance as a murder and go talk to those fishermen.

The government tracked the fishermen down. Only one was still alive. They interviewed him. Lin had said that particular fisherman was not one of the aggressors — he was actually the only one in the group who tried to save Zu, even though she did get killed.

When the government talked to him, he confirmed that yes, unfortunately Zu was killed. He had tried to stop it but couldn't. This fisherman had not been given any information — it was a brand new investigation, and he had no idea what they knew. But he basically exactly mirrored what Lin had said happened.

Based on this, the government closed Zu Shua-Wa's case. It was relabeled from a disappearance to a murder. The fishermen — by then deceased — were labeled as her killers. The case was closed based entirely on a reincarnated spirit's testimony.

There's one more thing. Back when Wu was hustling home, biking as fast as he could to get to his sick wife, and had those weird encounters with his neighbors — when those neighbors were interviewed, they said the reason they had given those looks of disgust was this: they knew Wu was married to Lin and that she was sick in bed. But here came Wu pedaling up the road with this woman sitting on the back of his bike — a woman who clearly was not his wife. Their reaction was that Wu was having an affair on his sick wife. What a horrible person.

Wu swore there was no woman on his bike. But he recalled the bike had felt really heavy, like he was pulling extra weight at the end.

Later, Lin would say that Zu's spirit had come down and hopped on the back of Wu's bike and was the woman the neighbors were seeing. Wu was so focused on getting home that even though he felt the heavy weight, he never turned around. Once Lin passed away, Zu's spirit — already at the home, waiting — switched places with her.

For the rest of her life, Lin would live as Zu. She became a community and spiritual leader. She passed away in 2018 at the age of 97. At her funeral, banners were hung that actually said both names: Lin and Zu.

If reincarnation is real, this story might just be one of the most credible cases ever recorded.
