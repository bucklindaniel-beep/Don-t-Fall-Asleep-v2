## Story 01 — Christoforos Nicolaou / Gamer Lured into Dark Web Forum

What if I told you a 15-year-old kid sat down to play Grand Theft Auto one evening, saw a pop-up offering free in-game credits, clicked it, and that single click set off a chain of events that would end his life?

This was Christoforos Nicolaou — though we'll just call him Christopher, as everyone around him did. He was 15, from Waltham Cross in Hertfordshire, England, and by every account, this wasn't a troubled kid. He had an IQ of 140, sat at the top of his class, and had his whole future mapped out. He wanted to be an architect. His dad, George, described him as the kind of kid who lit up every room he walked into. His mom, Areti, said two days before everything went wrong, he was asking about flights to Greece and stressing over his upcoming exams.

In January 2022, Christopher was playing GTA when a pop-up appeared on his screen offering free in-game credits if he filled out a form. (When it comes to GTA getting pop-up ads, that's not really a thing. So either Christopher was playing on a custom server or simply had a virus.) He typed in his full name, his home address, his phone number, and his passport details. The pop-up redirected him straight into a dark web forum, and the second he hit submit, that was it.

Within moments, a message came through: complete a series of challenges, otherwise your family gets killed. To an adult, that sounds like an obvious scam. But Christopher was 15, and these people now had his home address, his parents' names, and his passport number. He believed something bad was going to happen.

The challenges started small — almost laughably small. Eat a bowl of cereal within a set time frame. Run backwards up the stairs. Then they escalated: stay awake all night, watch horror films until morning, film the inside of the entire house with his phone. And eventually his own body. They were also sending him pictures of knives, asking which one they should use to hurt his family.

This went on for 50 days, and the entire time, Christopher told nobody. His parents noticed something was off — he got quieter, withdrawn, visibly frightened — but he didn't tell them anything. He felt like he was being watched around the clock.

One of the final challenges investigators believe he was given was to deactivate all the security cameras in the house.

On the evening of March 1st, 2022, at 8:21 p.m., Christopher submitted his school homework online. At 9:25 p.m., he asked his dad if he could play for a bit. By 9:35 p.m., he was gone. His parents found him with an earpiece still in his ear and his phone on the floor. The moment they picked up the phone, a message came through on the screen that said: "Are you dead?"

When police returned his phone and computer, they said they couldn't find anything. It took George himself 10 minutes to find the Discord account. There was one IP address. Investigators believe Christopher may have been talking to a single predator playing multiple characters, possibly part of a larger organized network known as COM (short for community) — groups of online actors who recruit members and coerce victims as young as nine into harmful acts for clout. The NCA has described these networks as one of the UK's biggest criminal threats.

Nobody has ever been charged for what happened to Christopher.

At 11:06 a.m. after Christopher died, someone remotely accessed his Microsoft OneDrive and deleted everything.

George and Areti now run the Christoforos Charity Foundation, going into schools and talking to kids and parents about exactly this — because the thing that keeps George up at night now is how easy it was. Just one pop-up, and then they owned his boy.

## Story 02 — Two US Navy Servicemen / Dark Web Fentanyl

It's funny how little we worry about the logistics when we shop online. Most people are fine not knowing the where or how of their delivery — just a seller name or a tracking number is usually enough.

For two US Navy officers stationed in Kingsland, Georgia, what arrived in their mailbox in October 2017 came disguised as something completely ordinary. The packages were labeled East Van Echo Tours, like a travel company. Inside was fentanyl, and both men died from it.

The operation behind their packages had been hiding in plain sight for years. Thomas Michael Federic, 59, lived in West Vancouver, British Columbia. His alleged partner, Paul Anthony Nichols, aged 44, a British citizen, was also living at a West Vancouver address despite the fact that his visa had already expired.

Together they allegedly ran one of the more sophisticated dark web drug trafficking operations law enforcement had seen out of Canada, operating under the vendor name Canada One. They were importing drugs sourced from China and Hungary, then distributing them across the United States through dark web marketplaces — specifically Dream Market, where buyers and sellers used cryptocurrency and Bitmessage accounts to keep everything anonymous. The whole setup ran under two fake business names: East Van Echo Tours and Bridge City Consulting LLP — shell identities built specifically to make drug shipments look like paperwork.

The first package left Canada on September 27th, 2017, and was delivered in Georgia on October 11th. One Navy officer was dead shortly after. The second package went out October 6th and arrived October 13th. The second sailor died the same way. Both traced back to the same source.

The packaging found at the scene was a direct match for shipments coming out of West Vancouver, which happened to already be on the RCMP's radar for suspected drug trafficking. The two investigations merged. The Americans brought in the DEA, Homeland Security, the Naval Criminal Investigative Service, the FDA's Criminal Division, and the US Postal Inspection Service. The Canadians had the RCMP's Federal Serious and Organized Crime Unit and the Calgary Police Service. They called it Operation Canada One.

Over the following months, the RCMP intercepted several packages being shipped from the same address. In March 2018, they moved in. Officers arrested both Federic and Nichols at the West Vancouver property and seized approximately 1.6 kg of fentanyl with a street value estimated at around $3 million. They also found mail tracking slips at the residence that matched the exact packaging recovered by American authorities at the scene of the two deaths.

Nichols was deported back to the UK immediately since he'd been in Canada illegally. For Federic, it wasn't until May 24th, 2022, that he was arrested on a provisional US arrest warrant. Nichols was picked up in the UK the same day. Both faced extradition to stand trial in a Georgia federal courtroom, charged with conspiracy to import controlled substances, conspiracy to distribute, and money laundering. If convicted, they're looking at a minimum of 10 years up to life.

## Story 03 — Rie Isogai / Nagoya Underground Forum Murder

There's a certain kind of evil that doesn't come from one person alone. It gets built — meeting by meeting, message by message — until a group of people, some sick men in this case, talk each other into something they might not have done on their own. That's what happened in Nagoya, Japan, in the summer of 2007, and it started with a single post on an underground cell phone forum.

On August 17th, a 40-year-old unemployed man named Kenji Kawagishi — who was living out of a van — posted a message on a shady mobile web forum used for exchanging criminal information. The message implied robbery and that he was looking for partners.

Three men responded:
- Tsukasa Kanda, 36, a newspaper salesman from Gunma Prefecture
- Yoshimoto Hori, 32, an unemployed darts player buried under 4 million yen in debt
- Yujiro Hondo, 29, who couldn't make rent

All four were desperate for cash, so they met in person on August 21st to figure out how to get it.

From the very first message, these men were performing for each other. Kanda told Kawagishi he'd committed homicide twice before. Hori wrote in an email that he'd just gotten out of prison. Both were lies, told to seem harder, more dangerous, more committed than they actually were. Nobody wanted to be the one to back down.

Their first robbery attempt failed. A second attempt — breaking into a plumber's office — fell apart when Kawagishi panicked and ran, leaving Hondo behind to get arrested.

By the afternoon of August 24th, Kawagishi, Kanda, and Hori were back together in a video rental shop parking lot in Midori, Nagoya, with nothing to show for any of it. That's when Kanda proposed something worse: kidnap a woman walking alone, rob her, then kill her so she couldn't identify them. The other two agreed.

That evening the three men drove slowly through the Jiyugaoka neighborhood of Chikusa in Nagoya, searching for a target. Around 10:00 p.m. they spotted Rie Isogai, a 31-year-old office clerk walking home alone on a dark street. Kawagishi got out of the van, approached her pretending to ask for directions, and forced her inside. They handcuffed her and threatened her with knives. They drove to a remote parking lot and forced her PIN out of her.

Just after midnight, Kawagishi attempted to assault her, and Kanda and Hori actually stopped him — but the attempt terrified Isogai so badly she tried to escape the van. That's when they decided she had to die.

Despite her begging for her life, at around 1:00 a.m. they wrapped packing tape around her head. She was still alive. Kanda beat her skull with a hammer approximately 30 times while another strangled her with a rope. They dumped her body in a forest in Mizunami at 4:00 in the morning.

Then they went to try the ATM. It declined every attempt. Isogai had given them the wrong PIN. They split the 62,000 yen — roughly $400 — found in her handbag and made plans to do it again. That didn't happen because Kawagishi suddenly turned himself in, afraid of being executed. All three were arrested on August 26th.

At trial, none of them showed remorse. Kanda even started a blog claiming to tell the "real story" and had the nerve to call Rie Isogai a liar.

It's quite rare for a single murder to result in the death penalty in Japan, but the community felt so strongly that they gathered 333,000 signatures to ensure justice was served. On June 25th, 2015, Tsukasa Kanda was executed at the Nagoya Detention House.

## Story 04 — David Sonboly / Munich Mall Shooting Gun Sourced from Dark Web

On July 22nd, 2016, David Ali Sonboly opened fire at the Olympia shopping mall in Munich. Nine people were killed, 27 more were wounded. Almost all of his victims were young people with a migrant background. Then he turned the gun on himself. 28 people died just like that.

The gun — where did he get it from? In the US there's almost an automatic assumption that someone with a gun got it from a legitimate store. In this case, it was the opposite. Sonboly had found a seller on the dark web — a 33-year-old man named Philip Korber, who was living in Marburg, a small university city about 100 km north of Frankfurt.

The two connected through encrypted dark web forums, negotiated a price, and met in person twice — first to hand over a Glock 17, then again for 350 rounds of ammunition. The total transaction cost around 8,000 euros.

The background of these two men is also an equally weird part. Korber and Sonboly shared almost identical ideological views; both used a Nazi salute in their communications. Investigators even found a copy of Mein Kampf on Korber's computer. The judge at trial flatly described Korber as "certainly a racist." Sonboly had specifically chosen July 22nd because it was the fifth anniversary of Anders Behring Breivik's massacre in Norway. So when Korber's defense argued he had no idea what his buyer was planning, that landed pretty hollow in court.

Police caught up with Korber in August 2016, about a month after the shooting. They tracked him down through his dark web activity and ran a sting operation posing as buyers looking for an automatic weapon and another Glock 17. He took the bait, showed up, and was arrested. During the sting, he reportedly confirmed to undercover officers that he had been the one to sell Sonboly the gun.

Korber gave a full confession and expressed remorse. On January 19th, 2018, the Munich court sentenced him to exactly 7 years and 2 months — found guilty of nine counts of involuntary manslaughter, five counts of bodily harm, and illegal weapons dealing. It was the first time in German legal history that an arms dealer was held criminally responsible for a mass shooting he didn't personally commit.

But there was a third person in the story most people never heard about. The dark web forum where Korber and Sonboly connected in the first place had an administrator — a 31-year-old man who had built and run the site where illegal weapons and drugs were openly traded. In December 2018, a court in Karlsruhe convicted him on charges of negligent homicide, negligent bodily harm, and facilitating the illegal sale of weapons and drugs. He was sentenced to 6 years.

## Story 05 — Melody Sasser / Hitman-for-Hire Plot

Nobody died in this case, but it was a close shave, and the person who tried to make it happen is, well, a lover.

Meet Melody Sasser, 48 years old, from Knoxville, Tennessee. By all appearances, just a regular person. She had a hiking hobby, a Match.com profile, and apparently some very serious feelings about a man she met on that app — a guy referred to in court documents only as DW.

Sasser and DW connected on Match and became hiking friends. She helped him plan his Appalachian Trail hike, booking hostels, organizing rest stops, watching his vehicle while he was out on the trail — a level of investment that goes beyond friendship.

Then in the fall of 2022, DW called her with the news that he was engaged to someone else and moving to Alabama. Sasser's response, according to court documents, was: "I hope you both fall off a cliff and die."

Then she got to work.

She drove to the couple's Alabama residence. The new wife, referred to as JW, found that her car had been keyed. Then came threatening phone calls made using a device that disguised her voice from untraceable computer-generated numbers. Sasser was also tracking both of them through Strava — yes, the fitness app — which synced their smartwatches and broadcast their location and routes. She knew when JW went on a 2-mile walk.

When all that didn't work to end their love, she escalated. On January 11th, 2023, under the username Cat Tree, Sasser logged onto a dark website called Online Killers Market — a platform that allegedly offered hitmen for hire along with hacking, kidnapping, extortion, asset attacks, and sexual violence.

She uploaded a photo of JW, provided her name, her address, and paid the equivalent of $9,750 in Bitcoin with the instructions: "It needs to seem random or accident or plant drugs. Do not want a long investigation. She recently moved in with her new husband."

Then she waited. And waited. And got increasingly more frustrated. Two months and 11 days after placing the order, she messaged the administrator again and eventually agreed to pay more, thinking that would make the job get done faster.

What Sasser didn't know was that an outside law enforcement agency had already flagged the site and tipped off Homeland Security Investigations in Birmingham, Alabama, in April. Investigators pulled the messages, identified the target, and sent police patrols to JW's home in Prattville. When JW was told about the plot, she named Sasser immediately.

Sasser was arrested on May 18th, 2023. When investigators searched her home, they found a journal with a list of several other hitman websites written inside — in case this one didn't work. They also found a stack of cash sitting under a sticky note with a Bitcoin address on it.

In September 2024, Melody Sasser was sentenced to 100 months in federal prison and ordered to pay $5,389 in restitution directly to JW, who is alive — but now knows that someone paid nearly $10,000, tracked her fitness routes, photographed her, and spent months checking in on a dark website asking why she wasn't dead yet.

## Story 06 — Stephen Port / GHB Sourced from Dark Web

Between 2014 and 2015, four young men in East London were murdered in almost identical fashion: drugged with GHB, their bodies staged to look like accidental overdoses. All four had done one thing in common — they met Stephen Port through a dating app, went to his apartment in Barking, and never came home.

Port's weapon of choice was GHB (gamma-hydroxybutyric acid, also called G on the street). Colorless, nearly tasteless, and at the right dose, completely incapacitating. A teaspoon is enough to knock a person out. A few more drops can be lethal. That razor-thin line between unconscious and dead wasn't a problem Port was trying to avoid — according to survivors who later spoke to investigators, it was part of what he was after.

How did he get the drug? The dark web. He always sourced it through the dark web.

His first victim was Anthony Walgate, age 23, a fashion student Port had hired through an escort website in June 2014. Port gave him a fatal overdose of GHB, then dragged his body outside his apartment building and called emergency services claiming he'd seen a stranger collapse. When police looked into it, Port told them Walgate had taken the drug himself. That lie worked. He was convicted of a minor offense and released on bail.

Two months later, Port invited Gabriel Kovari, 22, a Slovakian man who had recently moved to London, to live in his flat. He drugged him, killed him, left the body in his apartment for several days, then moved it at night to a churchyard nearby. Police ruled it an accidental drug overdose.

Less than a month after that, 23-year-old Daniel Whitworth, a chef Port met on a dating site, was dead in the same churchyard, in the exact same spot as Kovari. Port had staged it with a planted fake note written as though Whitworth were confessing to accidentally killing Kovari and taking his own life out of guilt. Then Port created a fake Facebook identity and fed that suicide narrative directly to Kovari's ex-boyfriend, who passed it on to police as a witness. Officers believed it and closed the case.

Almost a year passed until September 2015, when Jack Taylor, 25, a forklift driver, met Port on Grindr. His body turned up in the same churchyard, same staging and method. It was Taylor's sisters who refused to accept the accidental overdose conclusion and identified Port from CCTV footage captured the night before their brother's body was found. That's what finally led to his arrest.

After the murders became public, investigators and journalists began uncovering that Port had been connected to an underground network operating on dark web platforms where GHB-spiking was a community. One survivor who spent months trying to infiltrate these networks after being spiked himself on a Grindr date found forums that took weeks to gain access to, requiring members to prove their trustworthiness before being admitted. What he found inside was a structured subculture where members shared techniques for administering GHB without detection — mixing it into lubricant, applying it through massage oil, vaporizing it in the air alongside crystal meth to lower the victim's defenses. They livestreamed victims losing consciousness and sold recordings. They shared methods for accessing victims' phones to collect blackmail material to keep survivors from going to the police.

Port was sentenced to life in prison with no possibility of parole in November 2016.

## Story 07 — Yevgenia Shishkina / First Documented Dark Web Contract Killing

Stories about cops making enemies on the job are not uncommon. But what happened to Lieutenant Colonel Yevgenia Shishkina in October 2018 outside her home near Moscow was something investigators had genuinely never seen before. It was considered the world's first documented contract killing ordered through the dark web.

Shishkina had been a police investigator since 1991. She'd worked her way up through the Russian Interior Ministry, specializing in economic crimes, fraud, and drug trafficking. She was relentless. Former colleagues described her as someone who didn't socialize at work, didn't take bribes, and didn't back down. Her son told the BBC she was "looking for her own kind of justice."

The man she had been hunting for years was a cyber fraudster named Yaroslav Sumbayev — a hacker from Astrakhan who, along with two accomplices, had stolen 17.5 million rubles from 31 travel agencies by infiltrating their ticketing systems and redirecting funds. Shishkina had been building the case against him since 2014.

When Sumbayev got wind of it, he cut off his house arrest bracelet and fled — first to Montenegro, then Turkey, then Georgia — living under a fake Lithuanian passport and landing on Interpol's database. From abroad, he didn't go quiet. He sent Shishkina taunting messages from anonymous accounts, called her from unknown numbers, wrote on forums mocking her. He even sent someone to torch her car. Her bosses offered her a security detail; she turned it down.

Sumbayev had stopped being just a hacker on the run. By the time he was hiding in Georgia, he had built something much larger. Under the pseudonym Miguel Morales — borrowed from the name of a notorious Mexican cartel leader — he ran a dark web drug retail operation called Los Zetas on Russia's largest illegal trading platform, Hydra. In under two years on the platform, the store completed more than 250,000 transactions. He had deputies, cashmen, regional coordinators, and a structured operation that investigators estimated brought in tens of millions of rubles a month.

In August 2018, a 17-year-old high school student from St. Petersburg, identified in court documents only as G, posted on Hydra looking for work. He was already one of Los Zetas' street-level coordinators. A month after his post, someone reached out through private messages. The offer: 1 million rubles — roughly £12,000 — to have a police investigator killed. Shishkina's name, home address, and personal details arrived via Telegram.

G kept 400,000 rubles and brought in his neighbor, 19-year-old Abdulaziz, a first-year medical student he'd met on a basketball court. Abdulaziz thought it over for two weeks, then agreed. He told G the reason he was traveling to Moscow was to attend a concert by his favorite rapper. He bought his outfit for the killing at a discount store named Season. He learned to load the gun by watching YouTube videos in his hotel room. Then he traveled from St. Petersburg to Moscow on a BlaBlaCar rideshare. He retrieved the murder weapon — a modified pistol — from a stash hidden in a wooded area outside the city, the location sent to him by G over Telegram. He booked a hotel in Krasnogorsk on Avito and paid with a credit card.

On the morning of October 10th, 2018, at 6:30, Abdulaziz approached Shishkina outside her apartment building. She spotted him drawing the weapon and began fighting back. He slipped, caught himself on his left hand, and fired from the hip into her stomach. Then he got up and shot her in the neck. She died in her husband's arms.

Abdulaziz walked two blocks, ordered a Yandex taxi on his phone, and went to the concert. He posted a video from it on Instagram that night.

It was apparently the taxi record that unraveled him. Police pulled up every ride-hailing trip logged in the area that morning, traced it back to his phone, and 5 months later, both he and G were arrested in St. Petersburg. At their first interrogation, they described the entire chain — making this the first confirmed contract killing ever arranged and executed through the dark web.

Sumbayev was arrested in Georgia months later, carrying a fake passport and a pistol. He denied any connection to the murder.

## Story 08 — Stephen Allwine / Husband Used Dark Web to Murder Wife

Cottage Grove, Minnesota — a small town where nothing much happens that's worth the big news, and where a man could spend years building a reputation as a devoted husband and father. That was Stephen Allwine, and for a long time, it worked.

His wife, Amy Allwine, was a dog trainer, a mother deeply embedded in her church community, and people loved her. So when she started receiving anonymous death threats in 2016 — detailed, specific, relentless messages warning her that someone had been hired to kill her — she did exactly what you're supposed to do. She went to the FBI.

The messages were designed to psychologically destroy her. One read: "Commit. If you do not, then you will slowly see things taken away from you, and each time you will know you could have stopped it, which will eat you apart from the inside."

Investigators traced the threats to a dark website called Besa Mafia — a fake murder-for-hire platform that took large sums of Bitcoin from people who wanted someone killed, pocketed the money, and never sent anyone. Someone had paid thousands of dollars in cryptocurrency to have Amy gone. Because Besa Mafia was a scam, no hitman ever showed up. The investigation stalled, and Amy tried to move forward with her life — shaken but alive.

What she didn't know was that the person who had paid for her death was sitting right across from her at the dinner table.

On November 13th, 2016, Stephen Allwine called 911 in a trembling voice to report he had come home from running errands with their 9-year-old son to find Amy dead in their bedroom with a 9 mm pistol near her body. He told dispatchers it looked like she had shot herself. Given the threats she had been receiving, he suggested the fear had finally broken her.

Investigators were skeptical within minutes. The gun had been placed in Amy's non-dominant hand, and there was no gunpowder residue on either hand — meaning she hadn't pulled the trigger. Not a single drop of blood was on the weapon, and someone had cleaned up a pool of blood at the scene before officers arrived.

The toxicology report came back: Amy's system contained scopolamine, a drug that induces heavy sedation and confusion, bought by Stephen online. Investigators believe he drugged her first, then staged the entire scene.

When FBI forensic analysis pulled the Bitcoin transaction records, the trail from Besa Mafia led directly back to Stephen's IP address. The threatening messages Amy had been receiving for months — the ones that had sent her to the FBI in terror — Stephen had written every single one of them. He had spent months making his own wife believe a professional killer was coming to kill her, while simultaneously trying to make that actually happen.

The motive presented at trial had two layers. Privately, Stephen had been an active user of Ashley Madison, an affair website. He was living a completely separate life behind the image of a devoted church deacon. There was also a more straightforward financial calculation: Amy had a $700,000 life insurance policy, and Stephen was the sole beneficiary.

In January 2018, the jury convicted him of first-degree premeditated murder. He was sentenced to life in prison without the possibility of parole.
