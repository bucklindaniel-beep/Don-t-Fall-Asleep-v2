## Story 01 — Trapped in the Fire (Damonte Cheryl and Ruben Holmes)

On May 18th, 2024, at 9:00 a.m., residents of Charlotte, North Carolina, woke to black smoke rising from a seven-story apartment complex under construction at 7741 Liberty Road Drive. The building was not yet occupied by residents, but 17 construction workers had clocked in that morning. The structure was primarily wood and insulation on its upper five floors, making it highly flammable. By the time firefighters arrived, the fire had already grown substantially.

Firefighters entered and led a group of employees to safety. Once outside, they realized they had only found 15 of the 17. Two men — 30-year-old Damonte Cheryl and his coworker Ruben Holmes — were still inside.

Damonte opened Facebook and began a live stream from inside the building. The footage showed the two men on the sixth floor, the second-highest floor, surrounded by thick black smoke. Though the fire had not yet reached them directly, the smoke made breathing difficult and visibility nearly impossible. A large crane with a rescue basket was deployed but was unable to locate them through the smoke. At one point the crane operator believed he spotted the men, but the intensity of the blaze forced the operator himself to be rescued.

The only access point to floor six was a single stairway on the opposite side of the building. That path had been blocked, and jumping from six stories into the heart of a fire was not survivable. Rescue teams climbed the stairway and began moving toward the men, guided by their shouting. Before they could reach them, the order was given to evacuate the structure immediately due to imminent collapse risk. Firefighters called out one final time. The men did not come.

The building fell silent. The fire consumed the entire structure. Interior temperatures reached 2,000 degrees Fahrenheit before it collapsed. Damonte and Ruben were the only fatalities.

Damonte's live stream had been watched in real time by his friends, family, and his mother. His brother arrived at the scene during the event and pleaded with firefighters to watch the footage. Investigations following the fire revealed that the construction crew had been applying organic foam insulation, a highly flammable material that produced thick black smoke. The machine spraying the insulation caught fire, triggering the blaze. No smoke detectors or alarms had been installed in the building at the time. A temporary stairway had recently been removed from the section where the two men were working, eliminating what would have been an additional exit.

The companies involved were fined $50,000 and are currently being sued by the victims' families.

## Story 02 — Johnny Cashman

Johnny Cashman was 38 years old, lived alone in Lynchburg, Virginia, and spoke with his family nearly every day. After six days without contact, his family called the Lynchburg police and requested a welfare check. On April 19th, 2022, officers entered his apartment and found him dead. The cause was determined to be a gastrointestinal bleed, classified as a medical emergency. No autopsy was performed. Officers told the family there was no evidence of trauma, no drugs, and nothing suspicious. Johnny's body was cremated.

Ten days after the discovery, Johnny's ex-girlfriend and her mother returned to the apartment to collect belongings. They found blood throughout — pooled in the bathroom, wiped on doors, spread across the living space, on a chair and a banister, and splattered across the walls with long streaking handprints. The scene did not match what they had been told.

The neighbor across the hall had a security camera facing Johnny's door. The footage showed Johnny arriving home at approximately 3:03 p.m. — holding an empty glass, punching in his door code, and entering normally. Moments later, sounds of a physical struggle were audible from inside, including shouting and loud thuds. The unit went quiet. Seven minutes after Johnny entered, a second man emerged from the apartment. He was not Johnny. As he moved toward the building exit, he appeared to change his mind, turned back, and touched the door handle only with his sleeve, as though wiping it clean. He then stopped again at the base of the stairs, appearing visibly shaken, before composing himself and leaving. One minute after the unidentified man left, Johnny could be heard calling for help from inside the apartment. Then silence.

Police had this footage from the day of the incident. Johnny's death remained classified as a natural medical emergency. When the family later raised the footage publicly, investigators acknowledged their original account was incomplete and revised it — stating that the blood spread was consistent with uncontrollable vomiting and that Johnny had likely suffered for some time, contradicting the earlier claim of instant death.

In May 2022, the unidentified man was identified as Steven Church. He told police he was a transient who had met Johnny in Lynchburg after a job opportunity fell through. Johnny had allowed him to stay on his couch. On the day in question, Steven claimed Johnny arrived home drunk and began arguing with him about rent. He said the argument was verbal only, and that he left immediately. He stated he returned to wipe his fingerprints because he was afraid of being blamed given his prior felony record. He said he saw Johnny hunched over as if about to vomit but claimed to have left before any medical event began.

Investigators noted that the audio from the neighbor's footage did not contain anything resembling a verbal argument about rent. The phrases audible are more consistent with a physical confrontation than a verbal dispute. In October 2022, the case was officially closed. Steven Church was cleared. The cause of death remained classified as a medical emergency. The primary supporting evidence was that Steven's shoe prints were not found in the blood, only Johnny's. No autopsy had been performed and none was now possible.

## Story 03 — Bethany Martin

On a warm July morning in 2019, 17-year-old Bethany Martin and an unnamed 16-year-old friend were walking to a gas station near their home in San Antonio, Texas. Their path passed a drainage ditch where they noticed what appeared to be a man crouching against the concrete wall. As they approached, they realized he was not moving and was in an unnatural position. The man was 25-year-old Marcus Adams, who had taken his own life earlier that morning.

After calling the police and being sent on their way, the two girls returned home, set up a camera, and filmed a TikTok — positioning themselves on a couch and captioning it with text indicating they had found a dead body that morning. The post drew mixed responses. Some sympathized; others found it insensitive.

What emerged the following day revealed something more serious. Before police had arrived, Bethany and the 16-year-old had filmed a second video. In it, Bethany approached the body and removed a necklace from the deceased — a medallion the younger girl said matched her style. The chain was tossed into the nearby grass. The 16-year-old posted this footage to her Snapchat story. The audio on the video clearly indicates the girls were in good spirits during the act.

Someone who saw the Snapchat story downloaded the footage and posted it unedited to a neighborhood app called Next Door. Through this reposting, multiple members of Marcus Adams's family saw the footage — including some who did not yet know he had died.

Both girls were questioned by police. Bethany was charged with theft of a human corpse. She was not sentenced to significant jail time. The 16-year-old's charges were not publicly released. The medallion was returned to the family.

In a later TikTok, Bethany returned to present her account, describing herself as a victim of pressure from her friend, who she claimed had initiated and encouraged both the theft and the TikTok post. This account was not well received.

## Story 04 — Janelle Matthews

On December 20th, 1984, 12-year-old Janelle Matthews disappeared from her home in Greeley, Colorado. She had attended a school choir performance that evening and was dropped off at home around 8:15 p.m. by a friend, as her parents were both elsewhere. She called her father on the phone and told him she had arrived safely. Approximately one hour later, her father returned to find the garage door open and the house empty. Janelle was gone, leaving behind her winter coat and shoes. In the snow outside, footprints were found leading to one of the home's windows, consistent with someone having stood there watching the house.

Janelle's disappearance received significant national attention. Her image was placed on milk cartons across the country, making her one of the first milk carton kids. President Ronald Reagan referenced her case in a speech. Despite this coverage, no resolution came for over three decades.

In July 2019, a construction crew installing a pipeline in an isolated field in Weld County, Colorado, unearthed the remains of a child. The remains were confirmed to be Janelle's. She was still wearing the clothes from her recital. She had been killed by a single gunshot wound to the head, almost certainly on the night she disappeared.

At the time of her disappearance, a man named Steven Panky had been living in the same area of Colorado. After the crime, he relocated to Idaho without apparent reason. In 2019, while running for governor of Idaho, Panky published a Facebook post about Janelle's case, calling for a fresh investigation and arguing that law enforcement had been wrong to focus on a kidnapping and murder theory.

Months after posting this, Panky was arrested for the abduction and murder of Janelle Matthews. Investigators believed he had first noticed Janelle while working as a janitor at her church. Evidence supporting the case was primarily circumstantial: the day after the disappearance, Panky took his wife on an unplanned trip to California with no prior notice; his car subsequently caught fire and was taken to a scrapyard before any investigation could examine it; he began obsessively following news coverage of the case, having his wife read every article to him repeatedly; he made frequent unprompted calls to police providing leads and claiming to fear he would be blamed despite claiming never to have known Janelle.

During some of these calls, he reportedly provided details about the disappearance that had not been made public. Years later, following the death of his son Carl in 2008 — a murder unrelated to Panky, fully solved — Panky was seen at Carl's memorial sobbing and saying aloud that he hoped God had not allowed Carl's death because of what he had done to Janelle Matthews.

Steven Panky maintained his innocence throughout. He was convicted and sentenced to life in prison with the possibility of parole in 2040. The conviction came 12,634 days after Janelle's disappearance.
