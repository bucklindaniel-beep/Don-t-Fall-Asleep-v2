### Story A — Joseph Parker, Springville, Tennessee (2014)

911, location of your emergency.

It's the 7th of November, 2014. 45-year-old Joseph Parker dials 911 at 2:45 in the morning to report an urgent incident that had taken place inside his home in Springville, Tennessee. An incident that had been solely his own doing.

"I'd been married 12 years — on the 4th, which would have been two nights ago at 4:00 AM. I shot my wife in the temple of her head. And I thought I'd killed her and I put her in the freezer out in the garage."

It happened on him and his wife Samantha's 12th wedding anniversary — a day that should have been a celebration, but instead had somehow ended in Joseph shooting Samantha at point blank range before proceeding to put her body inside their garage freezer. An awful crime that Joseph was now confessing to a 911 operator. But this confession wasn't the real reason for his call. As Joseph likely would have been content without ever reporting his own crime, had it not been for the fact that hours prior, he noticed something unusual.

"I was in the garage when I checked on her that night and she's not dead. She's got a big hole in the temple of her head and to get her body moved around in there, I think I broke her wrist. She was frozen from being in the freezer for 48 hours."

According to Joseph, Samantha was still alive, despite having a large hole in her head and spending so long in their freezer that she was seemingly frozen solid. When he checked on her that morning, he realized that she wasn't actually dead and was even still conscious. The scenario Joseph was describing should have been impossible, with it being so outlandish that Joseph felt the need to tell the operator that this wasn't a prank.

"This is no prank call. I need somebody to get out there and help her. I promise you this is a legit call and I need somebody to get out there and help her, because I still love her. It's hard to believe that after that, you know, but I love her. I still love her. I've loved her every day I've been married to her."

He was being serious, and all he wanted to do now was get Samantha help, as he claimed to still love her despite everything he had done. And help couldn't come quickly enough as Joseph went into greater detail about just how bad Samantha's condition was and how he was able to determine she was alive in the first place.

"She's been frozen for two days. She's frozen solid. It's amazing she's still alive. She just called her jaw. She's in bad shape. She's a mess. She can't talk. I just — blink. Blink once, yes. Blink twice, no."

Despite being unable to speak or move, Samantha was still blinking and was actually blinking in response to questions Joseph was asking her — once for yes and twice for no. And in a moment of what appears to be regret for what he had done, he tried to make things better by giving her blankets and pouring water in her mouth.

"Yes, she's still in the garage. I made her as comfortable as I can, made her try to drink a little water."

These responses led to a lengthy one-sided conversation that ended with Joseph vowing to get her help, which he was attempting to do now as he drove miles away, clearing the scene for authorities and paramedics to save her, while calling 911 and explaining the surreal scenario.

"We don't have a history of domestic disputes. You know, we've never had to call police or nothing. We just had a real bad night a couple of nights ago. Waving the gun around, got myself in trouble."

The two had a happy and healthy marriage that for whatever reason had turned sour on their anniversary, and something in Joseph had snapped. But despite admitting to the murder, he made it clear that he had no intention of going to prison. He just wanted to make sure that Samantha was saved.

"Don't want to get arrested tonight and all that."

— telling the operator that they'll find her in the freezer before ending the call shortly after.

"Go to the garage. She can't yell — you go to the garage, that's where you'll see her once you get to the garage."

Sure enough, when authorities entered Joseph and Samantha's home and ventured into the garage, they would see Samantha's red hair flowing out from the side of the freezer. And inside they would find Samantha herself — frozen completely solid with a gunshot wound to her head in the same exact manner that Joseph had described. But other than this, it was clear that Joseph's retelling of events was flawed, to say the least. As Samantha hadn't just been placed inside the freezer — she had been jammed inside of it, as if he was desperate to get her to fit inside, so much so that he actually sawed off her left leg along with making a series of other cuts to her body to get her to fit. And this wasn't even the most damning revelation, with the most important having to do with Samantha's condition. Just by looking at her, it was clear to the first responders that Samantha was dead and had been dead for days, likely having been killed instantly by the gunshot, meaning that the blinking Joseph had witnessed was entirely hallucinated. It never could have happened. And yet that was his reality. He fully believed it.

Upon the discovery, Joseph quickly became the number one most wanted man in Tennessee. And that same day, police would track down his vehicle as he appeared to have been trying to flee the state. But the officers got to him first, pulling him over and then surrounding his vehicle, drawing their guns, and preparing for anything. But just as Joseph had said on the phone, he had no interest in going to jail. And that was something he was dead serious about. When officers finally got his car door opened, they would find Joseph Parker in the driver's seat, dead from a self-inflicted gunshot wound to the head.

His death made it impossible to fully confirm why he had taken Samantha's life that night. But reports seem to indicate that he had recently lost his job, and the couple was experiencing financial hardships, putting their home in danger of foreclosure — with this perhaps being the reason the two were arguing on their anniversary. But reports also indicate that this murder didn't happen in the midst of the fight, as evidence points to Samantha having been asleep at the time she was shot, adding some uncertainty to how the situation truly did unfold. But one noteworthy discovery made after the fact was that at the time of his death, Joseph had drugs in his system — which could have been a factor in the murder itself, and it certainly was a factor in the hallucinations he was seeing afterwards, as he stood in his garage having full conversations with the frozen, mutilated corpse of the woman he had just killed, all the while truly believing it was all real.

### Story B — Jennifer Sheffield, Brandon, Mississippi (2024)

Jennifer Sheffield's life was largely encapsulated by the posts she made on social media. She worked hard studying in medical school with the hope of becoming a doctor, a journey that she documented on her growing TikTok page. But when she wasn't at school or at a hospital, her time was spent with her family. She had been married to a man named Brandon Sheffield for around a decade. And when they met, Brandon already had two children from a separate relationship. Though this didn't deter her in any way, and instead she took on the responsibility of helping to care for them, raising the children alongside Brandon as if they were her own for much of their lives, to the point where they seemingly started referring to her as their mother — a role that she thrived in, as she frequently posted pictures and videos alongside them. And the same was also true for her being a wife, as she loved to share her memories and moments with Brandon, cultivating this image of the perfect family and the perfect pair.

But on the 10th of December, 2024, Jennifer would drop a bombshell on her followers.

"So, I'm about to be a newly single mom. And I am in the middle of a divorce. My husband is being incredible, though. Like, really and truly — we are working together. Everything's great. We're splitting everything down the middle. You know, everything is good."

After approximately 10 years of marriage, Jennifer announced that she and Brandon were getting a divorce. There was no explanation for why, just the simple fact that they were separated and had already started the divorce process — something that was reiterated by both of their Facebook relationship statuses as they shifted to single around the same time. Despite the sad and even shocking news, it was clear that they were handling it better than most. There didn't appear to be any resentment between them, as Jennifer described Brandon as having been incredible throughout the process, supporting her in any way possible.

"It is a very civil divorce with our daughter, our daughter in mind, and I really appreciate that. However, I'm going to be a mom, single income right now, and he will be helping with her. He will be supporting her. So essentially, I'm really thankful for that and that I will not have to worry so much about supporting her."

It was apparent that there was some kind of custody split being discussed between them. But what was clear was how grateful Jennifer was for Brandon's support. But even with this help, this marked the first time in Jennifer's adult life that she was really on her own.

"I've never done this before. I've never been on my own before. So yeah, if you just have any tips, like I would really appreciate it, because I'm a little scared right now."

It was uncharted territory and something that she was understandably nervous about. But despite her fears, Jennifer maintained an optimistic attitude, and thankfully at this point the divorce itself was going about as well as it could be. They were both supportive of each other. They were both thinking of the children. And there seemed to be no bad blood between them.

18 days after posting this video praising Brandon Sheffield for the incredible way he had been handling their divorce, both Jennifer and Brandon would be found dead inside their Mississippi home. That morning, family members arrived at the house to perform a welfare check, leading to them calling the police. Inside, officers discovered the pair lying next to each other. Each had a single gunshot wound to the head, with one thing being blatantly obvious: Brandon Sheffield had been the one to pull the trigger both times — shooting Jennifer before turning the gun on himself.

What happened that night isn't fully understood, but the motive is fairly clear. This was likely the result of the divorce that Brandon had supposedly been handling so well, though obviously there seemed to be more going on behind the scenes. Perhaps the violence was spurred on by the holiday season and Brandon's realization that this would be their last as a married couple. Or perhaps he just wanted Jennifer all to himself, and if he couldn't have her, no one could.

Since her murder, Jennifer's video has become a morbid piece of internet history. And just seeing how full of hope she was is heartbreaking in hindsight. Because of Brandon's actions, not only was Jennifer robbed of her future, but these children were robbed of their mother figure and their father figure all in one night. And making this even more devastating, some articles have claimed that one of these children was actually in the house during the shootings. What Brandon did was nothing short of evil.

"I had this feeling this morning, for some reason, to tell you that it's going to be okay. You still have a purpose. You've got to keep pushing. You still have a heartbeat. And that means that you've got to keep going."

It was unlike anything she had posted before.

### Story C — Rana Ibrahim, Grenfell Tower Fire, London (2017)

Throughout 31-year-old Rana Ibrahim's Facebook page, friends, family, and followers had been accustomed to seeing posts of her children, various religious texts, and other media from her day-to-day life. But on June 14th, 2017, Rana would begin her very first live stream. And it was clear from the start that something had gone terribly wrong.

"Don't open the front door."
"Open the front door — you're going to bring the smoke in. You're not going to be able to breathe. I'm scared. Maybe someone outside."
"They're in the other — are you sure? They've gone into the apartment."

The footage is from Rana's perspective as she and another woman discuss what lies behind the front door of the apartment unit they were in. The woman, believed to be 33-year-old Hana Ismail, told her not to open the door as it would let the smoke in, while Rana insisted that people might still be out there and in danger — eventually leading to her peeking outside and giving us a glimpse inside their nightmare situation.

"There's too much smoke here."
"Okay. Okay. I'm going to open it."

The door opens for just a split second, revealing a thick layer of black smoke, the loud ringing of fire alarms, and someone pounding on a door. Rana eventually calls out to whoever was knocking outside, beckoning them towards her.

"Hello, come here. Come here. Hello. Hello."
"Come, come, come. Quick, quick."

To which 57-year-old Gary Manders emerges from the smog completely covered in ash.

It was a dramatic scene and one that you can start to piece together based on the footage alone, as it was clear that there was a fire raging inside that apartment complex and the entire floor Rana was filming on had been filled with smoke — smoke that essentially trapped her and a few others inside this flat. Given the sheer amount of smoke in that hallway, it was obvious why the others in the room insisted on keeping that door closed. But Rana continued to hit back on this as she didn't want anyone to be stuck out there alone.

"Come in — because THE SMOKE IS COMING. THE PEOPLE DOWN. THE PEOPLE OUTSIDE. HELLO. HELLO. OKAY. I'm going out. Hello."
"Oh my god, I closed it."

Before eventually conceding and closing the door for good. It was obvious that the situation was dire, but just how dire wasn't revealed until Rana made her way over to the window. They were 23 stories in the air — on the highest floor of the Grenfell Tower, located in North Kensington, London, England — where Rana and hundreds of others lived. And now somewhere in that high-rise was a growing fire.

As she looks out the window, Rana tells her viewers that she believes the fire had started from the bottom of the structure and was quickly working its way up to them, with it now being just one floor below them. But officially, this wasn't the story that Rana and the others in the room were being told. Each time they would dial 999, the operators would tell them that the fire was generally contained and leave them with one clear instruction: Stay put.

This was the name of a nationwide rescue policy for all high-rise fires — that people should stay in their flats and wait for rescue, as opposed to trying to escape on their own. Buildings like these were made from thick concrete and should hypothetically keep fires relatively contained in the flat that they started in for an estimated time period of around two hours. And considering that this fire had started roughly one hour before Rana's stream, they should have been relatively safe until rescue crews got to them.

Approximately one hour earlier, on the fourth floor of the tower, a man called 911 to report that his freezer had caught fire. Due to his quick phone call, firefighters arrived within minutes. A firefighter wearing a camera showed that they were able to handle the blaze in a relatively efficient manner — using thermal imaging to confirm the fire was out. But the camera then panned to the window, revealing scorching hot droplets of debris falling from the outside, like molten rain. The fire had spread above them and was now burning up the side of the building — in a manner that was completely unprecedented based on the firefighters' knowledge of the scene. And by the time they even realized what was happening, things were already out of hand.

Within just that first hour, nearly the entire building was engulfed in flames. Rana was trapped on the tallest floor. Over a hundred people were still in the building at this time. Two of them were Rana's own children: four-year-old Fathia and three-year-old Hana, stuck in the same flat as their mother and several others. Some of the others called out the window for help.

"We're stuck on the 23rd floor. Hello."
"There's too many people stuck upstairs."

— until the stream went dark, roughly six minutes later.

Despite things looking grim, those in the group were told once again by 999 operators — even 30 minutes after the stream had ended — to stay put, with the operator claiming the fire was contained to the fourth floor. Rana's sister later spoke to the media, recalling that Rana did not try to leave because she respected and trusted and believed that they were coming to rescue them. And by that point there were hundreds of rescuers on the scene along with multiple helicopters flying around. But internally, fire crews had been told not to go past the 12th floor shortly after Rana's stream ended — it was too dangerous. And the helicopters were police helicopters used only for surveillance. They were not equipped for rescue, nor were they ever considered for that purpose — getting too close would have blown more air on the fire and worsened the blaze.

Authorities finally told those inside to try to escape on their own. But by this point, two hours after the fire had started, the fire had completely taken over the structure. There was only one stairway, and it was now impossible to safely traverse for anyone on the higher floors. Even attempting it would have been a death sentence. The orders to shelter in place had cost them valuable time and any chance at leaving while they could. Rana, Fathia, Hana, and everyone else shown in the live stream would perish in what we now know as the Grenfell Tower fire — a fire that claimed 72 lives, one of the worst fire tragedies in Europe's history.

The Grenfell Tower was built in 1974. Around the 2010s its concrete exterior was considered outdated in an area experiencing a wave of modernization. The decision was made by the Kensington and Chelsea Tenant Management Organization and the Royal Borough of Kensington and Chelsea to add new cladding to the exterior. The cladding was produced by a company called Arconic. Arconic had tested it multiple times; it received an F grade for fire resistance. Internal emails acknowledged the failure — one reportedly followed the F-score with the word "Oops." Despite this, the product continued to be sold. Those in charge of the Grenfell refurbishment selected the cheaper, non-flame-retardant version of the product, saving approximately £5,000 (~$6,500 USD) over the flame-retardant alternative. Justice, as of the time of the broadcast, had not yet been served. Investigations ongoing. Trials not expected to begin for another full year.

### Story D — Lane Bryant Shooting, Tinley Park, Illinois (2008)

It was the usual start to the day for Roa McFarland, the 42-year-old manager of the Lane Bryant store in Tinley Park, Illinois. Lane Bryant had a habit of launching monthly clearance events. This day, February 2nd, 2008, was yet another one of these sales. She knew it would be a busy day. That morning only one worker was scheduled to open the store — something that Roa disagreed with, leading to her coming in to help even though she had the day off. She clocked in at 10:00 AM.

In the first 15 minutes or so of opening, only five people had entered the store: four female shoppers and a delivery man holding a stack of papers. The man approached them and made casual conversation. But after a few minutes he just lingered, never actually delivering anything. Roa found this strange — it was a Saturday, a day they typically didn't receive packages. Growing more suspicious, Roa quietly placed a call to a nearby Lane Bryant store, who confirmed that nothing was scheduled to be dropped off that day. But before she could process this information, an incident was already unfolding around her.

The delivery man pulled out a pistol and pointed it at the women, yelling at them to hand over the money from the register. He then rounded up Roa, her employee, and the four shoppers, demanding they give up their cash and jewelry. After collecting these, he forced the women to the back of the store, bound them with duct tape, placed them face down on the floor, and put underwear over their heads to obstruct their view. In the minutes following, two other women entered the store, were also robbed, and were forced into the back room — one of them struck in the face with his pistol, nearly knocked unconscious.

After subduing all the women in the back room, the whole operation seemed to grind to a halt. He was pacing the room, slowly losing his cool. He was visibly agitated. The time ticked on. Sensing his disorganization, Roa quietly slipped her hand free and called 911 through her Bluetooth headset.

"911 emergency."
"We're at—"

Despite the man being in the room with her, Roa managed to alert authorities to her exact location, telling them to hurry. In the background you can also hear the robber himself — his disheveled state apparent. The operator was on it, quickly dialing dispatch and telling Roa not to hang up.

"Stay on the line. Let me get you to Tinley Park — don't hang up."

Within just a split second, the call disconnected.

By some stroke of good fortune, there was actually an officer in that shopping center about 100 feet away. From the time that Roa made that call, the officer managed to arrive on the scene within just a few minutes. He rushed inside the store, made his way to the back room. Inside he found them. Six women with underwear over their heads, lying on the floor — each one having been shot execution-style just seconds after Roa's 911 call. It was a mass murder. But the killer was gone.

Authorities flooded the area. The shooter was described as a Black man with a husky build and a single braid falling to his right cheek that ended in green beads. This description came not from security footage — the store had no CCTV cameras at the time — but from a survivor. When the man began his executions, he moved to the employee that Roa had come in to help. When he fired, she moved at just the right moment, causing the bullet to graze her neck. She then played dead. He didn't notice. Her testimony is the reason the description of the killer exists.

CCTV footage from a neighboring store showed that just before Roa made that call, two large SUVs pulled up in front of the building. It is believed that both SUVs were involved — one potentially as a lookout, the other as a getaway car — explaining how the shooter disappeared so quickly. The store was located near the entrance of an interstate.

That was 18 years ago. Neither the shooter nor the drivers have ever been identified — marking a rare instance in which a mass shooting in America went unsolved. In total, the killer made off with approximately $200, including cash taken from the women. The store had no CCTV, the shooter never covered his face, and his DNA was discovered under one of the victim's fingernails. Despite survivor testimony, a detailed physical description, a voice recording, and DNA, the case remains unsolved.
