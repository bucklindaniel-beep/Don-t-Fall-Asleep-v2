## Story 01 — The 18 Ponds of the Dragon King (Fay, China)

**Setting:** Small village in China, adjacent to the 18 Ponds of the Dragon King hiking area; February 16, 2020 onward
**Subject:** Fay (39), village resident with childhood tumor history that left her risk-averse; husband, parents, sister
**Threat type:** Unexplained 17-day disappearance; locals attribute to area's haunting (spirits seeking to switch places with the living)
**Outcome:** Fay returned alive after 17 days, severely malnourished and dehydrated, having lost 60 lbs; no rational explanation; Fay still lives in the village

### Story Beat Map
1. Feb 16, 2020 morning: Fay leaves convenience store in bright red coat with snacks
2. Five villagers at trailhead call her by name and invite her to hike
3. Fay accepts — first time ever doing something outside her routine due to childhood tumor recovery anxiety
4. Group hikes to one of the 18 ponds; clearing, waterfall, picnic on shore
5. Fay feels intense fatigue, slumps, falls asleep
6. Wakes alone in darkness; five companions gone
7. Panics; stumbles down trail in pitch-black for hours
8. Reaches stream at dawn; sees path; descends mountain
9. Sees crowd of villagers near trailhead; waves
10. Crowd reacts with shock and horror, not relief
11. Sister informs her: she has been gone 17 days
12. Hospitalized — 60 lbs lost, severely malnourished and dehydrated
13. Reveals: she had spent the time with five companions she remembers in detail
14. Family reveals: those five named people have been dead for a long time
15. Security footage confirms Fay walked into trail alone — no companions visible
16. COVID-era checkpoints confirm she never left the 7.5-mile area
17. Search party with fireworks found nothing; Fay reports she heard/saw nothing
18. Local lore: ponds area is haunted; spirits seek to switch places with living people; eerie blue lights observed by locals
19. Crowd at trailhead was not a search party — a local saw her red coat from below and thought it was one of the haunting "lights" (a ghost)
20. Prior survival record in the area was 12 days (fit young man, barely survived); Fay survived 17 days in poor health
21. Fay's account has never changed

### Source-Truth Anchors
- Date: February 16, 2020
- Location: 18 Ponds of the Dragon King, China; 7.5-mile trail stretch
- Disappearance duration: 17 days
- Weight loss: 60 lbs
- Prior survival record in area: 12 days
- Visual identifier: bright red distinctive coat
- COVID checkpoints: independent geographic verification
- Security camera footage: confirms solo entry
- Local belief: spirits seek living to switch places with; eerie blue lights

## Story 02 — Lin Wong-Yao / Zu Shua-Wa Reincarnation Case (Taiwan, 1959–1962)

**Setting:** Rural town in Taiwan; spring 1959 illness; March 17, 1962 government investigation
**Subjects:** Wu Chiu (civil engineer, husband); Lin Wong-Yao (37, wife, illiterate, frail, dies and revives); Zu Shua-Wa (claimed prior identity of revived Lin, niece of unnamed army hospital chief physician); chief physician and his wife
**Threat type:** Claimed body-switching / reincarnation; supported by independent corroboration via fisherman testimony
**Outcome:** Government closes Zu Shua-Wa's missing-persons case as murder based on reincarnated testimony; Lin lives as Zu until dying in 2018 at 97; funeral banners bear both names

### Story Beat Map
1. Spring 1959, Taiwan: Wu Chiu, civil engineer, bikes home rushing because his wife Lin is sick
2. Bike feels unusually heavy as he nears home — like pulling extra weight
3. Two neighbors give him looks of disgust as he passes; he has no idea why
4. Arrives home; Lin pale and seriously ill (37, frail, prone to minor illness, but this is severe)
5. Three days of home care; fever doesn't break; Lin stops eating/drinking
6. Wu asks neighbor with donkey cart for help to hospital; neighbor reluctant due to same disgust look
7. After plea, neighbor agrees; cart ride takes hours
8. At hospital Lin slips into coma overnight, dies
9. Body brought home for traditional 7-day Taiwanese funeral
10. Wu and family/neighbors mourn for 3 days at the open coffin
11. Day 3 of funeral: gasps in room; Lin is sitting upright in the casket, alive
12. Lifted to bed; gestures for water; gradually recovers within hours
13. Next day: walking to temple; speed-walking pace exceeds athletic Wu
14. Accent has changed; voice doesn't sound like his wife
15. Personality changes accumulate:
    - Previously illiterate; now reads and speaks fluent Mandarin
    - Previously a meat-cooking enthusiast; now vegetarian
    - Speaks with authority on accounting and bookkeeping
    - Advises Wu to quit engineering and open a building materials shop
16. Wu opens the shop; they make more money than ever before
17. Story spreads nationally; Taiwanese government investigates
18. March 17, 1962: government sends for Lin; brought to army hospital
19. Lin meets chief physician; immediately calls him "Uncle" with recognition
20. Chief physician calls his wife in without explanation; Lin calls her "Auntie"
21. Lin reveals her real name is Zu Shua-Wa
22. Chief physician confirms his niece was named Zu Shua-Wa; missing during Chinese Civil War 1 year prior; presumed dead, body never found
23. Lin says Zu was robbed and murdered by a group of fishermen; spirit in purgatory until Lin's death allowed body-switch
24. Confirmed personality matches between Lin-as-Zu and the niece: vegetarian, accounting interest, Mandarin fluency, accent
25. Government investigates fishermen named by Lin; only one alive; he confirms what Lin said — that he tried to save Zu but couldn't stop the others
26. Fisherman had no prior information about the investigation
27. Case officially relabeled from disappearance to murder; closed based on reincarnated testimony
28. Retroactive explanation for the heavy bike: Zu's spirit had hitched a ride home with Wu, was the "woman" neighbors saw on his bike, then switched into Lin's body when Lin died
29. Lin lives the rest of her life as Zu; becomes community/spiritual leader
30. Dies 2018, age 97; funeral banners bear both names: Lin and Zu

### Source-Truth Anchors
- Year illness: spring 1959
- Wife's age at death: 37
- Funeral revival: day 3 of 7-day Taiwanese funeral custom
- Government investigation date: March 17, 1962
- Niece's death context: Chinese Civil War, 1 year prior to Lin's death
- Personality changes: literacy, language (Mandarin), diet (vegetarian), profession (accounting/bookkeeping)
- Independent corroboration: fisherman's testimony with no prior information
- Death of subject: 2018, age 97
- Funeral banners: both names (Lin and Zu)
