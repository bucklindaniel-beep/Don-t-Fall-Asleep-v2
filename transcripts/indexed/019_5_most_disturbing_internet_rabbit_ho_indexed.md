## Story 02 — Rosangela Almeida: Buried Alive in Brazil

**Entities:** Rosangela Almeida (37, deceased), Almeida family, Sonora Santana Cemetery staff, Brazilian hospital doctors
**Setting:** Brazil, Sonora Santana Cemetery — January 2018
**Status:** Officially ruled dead prior to burial; buried alive question remains disputed

**Event Sequence:**
1. January 2018 — Rosangela Almeida, 37, collapses; taken to hospital; suffers two cardiac arrests; pronounced dead January 28 after several days in ICU
2. Family holds memorial; she is buried in a wooden casket inside a solid concrete tomb
3. Day after burial — neighbors near cemetery report hearing banging and faint screams from graves; initially dismissed as children pranking
4. Sounds continue for approximately 11 days
5. Someone connects sounds to Rosangela's grave; family and neighbors alerted
6. Casket exhumed; Rosangela shows no signs of life but body described as warm to the touch by multiple witnesses
7. Additional observations reported: loosened casket nails, apparent wrist injuries, blood marks on coffin interior, cotton from nose and ears displaced, body decomposition level inconsistent with 11-day interval
8. Rosangela taken to hospital and pronounced dead a second time; reburied
9. Doctors maintain she was dead before burial; cite intensive monitoring and strong antibiotic use (explaining slower decomposition)
10. Mother and some community members dispute official account; case becomes major national news
11. Police investigators state they found no witnesses who personally heard the noises — characterize sounds as unverified rumor

**Observable Mechanics:**
- Enclosed concrete tomb with wooden inner casket limits air supply — survival beyond a few hours physiologically unlikely
- 11-day survival without water and with sustained physical exertion is not biologically supportable
- Warm-body reports are consistent with recent death, not necessarily recent survival
- Pre-existing fainting condition (lifelong) is factually documented — raises false-death possibility but does not confirm it
- Case remains publicly disputed with no definitive forensic resolution

## Story 05 — The Mad Gasser of Mattoon

**Entities:** Aleen Kearney (first documented victim), Mattoon Police Department, local media
**Setting:** Mattoon Illinois — September 1–mid-September 1944
**Status:** Unresolved; no perpetrator identified; mass hysteria theory favored by criminologists

**Event Sequence:**
1. September 1, 1944 — Aleen Kearney wakes at approximately 11:00 p.m. to sickly sweet odor; attempts to stand; collapses with paralysis in arms and legs; burning sensation in lips and throat; daughter reports same symptoms
2. Sister summons help; Kearney's husband (taxi driver) arrives, sees a tall thin figure in black outside bedroom window wearing what appears to be a gas mask and holding a tubular device; figure disappears into alleyway when pursued
3. Both victims recover without permanent injury
4. Over the following approximately two weeks, at least 29 officially reported similar incidents occur across Mattoon, primarily affecting women home alone or with family
5. Reported symptoms: nausea, vomiting, temporary paralysis, dizziness, disorientation, burning in throat or eyes; some cases require medical attention
6. No physical evidence found in any subsequent incident: no broken windows, no fingerprints, no residue; witnesses report shadows near ventilation openings but no confirmed presence
7. Community response escalates: citizens patrol streets with firearms; people sleep in shifts
8. Police publicly state attacks are not real — attributed to community war anxiety and collective nervous breakdown
9. Attacks cease almost immediately following the police statement
10. No perpetrator ever identified

**Observable Mechanics:**
- First incident (Kearney): physical witness to figure outside window is the strongest corroboration in the entire series
- All subsequent incidents lack physical evidence; symptoms consistent with acute anxiety and hyperventilation
- Chemical odors identified by police as common household sources (cleaners, furnace gas, industrial pollution from local plants)
- Mass psychogenic illness pattern: sensational newspaper coverage amplifies fear; reported symptoms are also anxiety symptoms; cessation follows authoritative denial
- Case remains suspended between genuine first incident and subsequent mass psychogenic spread
