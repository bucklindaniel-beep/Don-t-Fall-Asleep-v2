## Story 01 — John Colter: Blackfoot Pursuit

**Entities:** John Colter (born c. 1774, Virginia; died c. 1812–1813), John Potts (trapping partner, deceased), unnamed Blackfoot chief, Meriwether Lewis, William Clark, Manuel Lisa (fur trader)
**Setting:** Jefferson River area, present-day Montana — summer 1809; broader backstory 1803–1810
**Status:** Historical — Colter's survival confirmed; specific details of the pursuit (the run itself) disputed by Blackfoot oral tradition

---

**Background — Colter's Life Before the Pursuit:**

1. Born c. 1774 in Staunton's Draft, Virginia; raised on the frontier; no formal education; skilled hunter and wilderness survivor
2. By late 1803 — encountered Meriwether Lewis near Limestone (later Maysville), Kentucky while Lewis was preparing the westward expedition; enlisted in the US Army's First Regiment October 15, 1803 at the Falls of Ohio
3. Early in the expedition — earned a reputation as a disciplinary problem: excessive drinking, leaving camp to visit a whiskey shop, confined to quarters 10 days, court-martialed for threatening an army sergeant during a dispute; nearly removed from the expedition
4. Despite discipline issues — kept on the expedition for his exceptional hunting skill; fed the party when conditions made hunting difficult; trusted with solo scouting missions; helped identify mountain passes; recovered lost horses and supplies in the Bitterroot Mountains; aided communication with multiple native tribes using nonverbal signals
5. 1806 — at the Mandan villages (present-day North Dakota), on the expedition's return east, Colter requests permission to join two trappers heading back into the interior; Lewis grants an honorable discharge approximately two months early
6. 1807 — heading back toward civilization, Colter encounters fur trader Manuel Lisa organizing an expedition into the upper Missouri River country; joins the group; reunites with John Potts; helps build Fort Raymond at the confluence of the Yellowstone and Bighorn Rivers
7. October 1807–spring 1808 — sent by Lisa to travel approximately 500 miles through unmapped territory to advertise the trading post; crosses the Continental Divide; explores Jackson Hole and the Teton Range; believed to have reached Yellowstone Lake; observes and documents geothermal features (steaming pools, sulfuric waterways, bubbling mud); his account dismissed and mocked as "Colter's Hell" on return
8. Summer 1808 — wounded in the leg (bullet or arrow) during a Blackfoot attack while escorting Flathead hunters; survives after a Crow force intervenes
9. Fall 1808–1809 — resumes trapping with John Potts in the Three Forks area, Jefferson River region, Montana; both men fully aware of Blackfoot hostility in the territory

---

**Event Sequence — The Encounter and Pursuit (Summer 1809):**

1. Morning — Colter and Potts in a canoe on the Jefferson River checking overnight beaver traps; Colter hears a distant rumbling, suspects danger, urges retreat; Potts dismisses it as buffalo
2. Blackfoot warriors emerge from the riverbanks — approximately 800 men; hand signal from a warrior beckons them ashore; Colter complies immediately
3. A warrior approaches Potts's canoe and takes his rifle; Colter snatches it back and returns it to Potts; Potts then retreats from the bank by canoe and refuses to come ashore despite Colter's urging
4. An arrow is fired into Potts's leg; Potts raises his rifle and fires — kills one warrior; a volley of arrows from the group strikes and kills Potts
5. Potts's body is brought ashore; the warriors dismantle the remains; parts are thrown at Colter's feet
6. Blackfoot warriors strip Colter of all clothing, weapons, and footwear; a council is summoned to determine his fate; initial plan is to use him as a target for archery practice
7. A Blackfoot chief arrives and sizes Colter up; asks Colter if he is a fast runner — Colter lies and says he is not; the council convenes and decides to give Colter a head start and hunt him
8. Colter is led to open ground and given a head start of several hundred yards; he walks deliberately until he detects movement behind him, then sprints
9. The pursuit begins — approximately 600 Blackfoot warriors give chase; Colter runs barefoot across uneven terrain; rocks cut his feet; prickly pear tears his skin; he begins bleeding from the nose within minutes due to exertion
10. After several miles — sounds of pursuit thin; most warriors fall back; one warrior remains close, within approximately 20 yards, carrying a spear
11. Colter stops suddenly from a full sprint and turns to face the warrior with arms raised; the warrior attempts to halt and throw the spear simultaneously but stumbles from exhaustion; the spear strikes the ground and snaps; Colter grabs the sharpened end, dives at the warrior, and kills him; takes the warrior's blanket for cover and runs for the trees
12. Colter runs approximately 5 miles to the Madison River; finds a beaver lodge (a large driftwood structure over a beaver slide); submerges under it with only his face above water
13. Blackfoot warriors arrive at the river and search the bank for hours; some stand on the lodge itself; Colter remains motionless and is not discovered
14. After nightfall — warriors withdraw; Colter swims downstream, exits the river, and begins a 200-mile overland journey back to civilization
15. Walks primarily at night; crosses a snowcapped mountain in near-nothing clothing; sustains himself on roots (prairie turnip); travels approximately 11 days
16. Arrives at a trading post on the Little Bighorn River — exhausted, injured, near collapse

---

**Aftermath:**
17. Colter recovers; returns briefly to the region to help construct a fort near Three Forks in 1810; two more trapping partners killed by Blackfoot; Colter decides to leave the wilderness permanently
18. By end of 1810 — travels to St. Louis; marries a woman named Sally; buys a small farm near Miller's Landing (now New Haven), Missouri
19. Shares accounts of his wilderness years with William Clark; Clark incorporates the information into a map of the region that remains the most accurate available for approximately 75 years
20. Enlists again during the War of 1812 with Nathan Boone's Rangers; dies in his late 30s — either May 7, 1812 (jaundice, per some accounts) or November 22, 1813

**Historical Dispute:**
- A Blackfoot oral tradition describes the encounter differently: Colter was stripped and released as a warning to other trappers; the hunt never occurred; the more dramatic account was possibly embellished during Colter's 200-mile walk back
- What is agreed upon across both versions: Colter encountered the Blackfoot, lost Potts, was stripped, escaped, and walked approximately 200 miles without proper clothing, food, or weapons

**Observable Mechanics:**
- Colter's decision to understate his running ability to the chief was the critical deception — had the chief believed him to be fast, a different (likely fatal) fate may have been chosen
- Walking the head start deliberately instead of running immediately extended the gap before the chase — Colter understood that the moment he ran, the pursuit would begin
- Turning to face the last warrior was a forced choice at near-exhaustion — stopping removed the forward-motion disadvantage and used the warrior's own momentum against him
- Beaver lodge concealment worked because the search was surface-level; searchers were looking for movement and did not probe underwater structures
- 200-mile overland traverse in near-nudity represents the true survival achievement — the run was approximately 5 miles; the subsequent journey was approximately 195 miles
