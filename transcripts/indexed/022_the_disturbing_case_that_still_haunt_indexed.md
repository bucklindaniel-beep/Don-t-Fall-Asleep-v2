## Story 01 — The Kazinet Seven

**Entities:**
- Victims (all 15, from Mezhdurechensk): Dmitri, Nikolai, Vadim, Oleg, Vyacheslav, Roman, Viktor
- Perpetrators: Nikolai (police lieutenant, lead attacker), unnamed foreman (railway), Vasili (foreman's/local's brother), Yuri (Vasili's brother), Slava (Vasili's brother — later killed by Vasili; claimed as third attacker)
- Train crew: Vadim and Igor Supernenko (brothers, freight train engineers)
- Investigators: Vladimir Gusenov (investigator, Moscow), Yuri Stallarov (criminal prosecutor, Moscow)
- Political figures: Boris Yeltsin (Russian President — letter recipient)

**Setting:** Kazinet village, Siberia (Khakassia Republic, Russia) — August 14, 1989; trial 1995; retrial verdict 2001
**Status:** Resolved — convictions upheld on third investigation (2001); sentences widely criticized as inadequate

---

**Event Sequence:**

Phase 1 — Discovery (August 14, 1989):
1. 1:40 a.m., August 14, 1989 — freight train #2008, 70 cars, operated by brothers Vadim and Igor Supernenko, approaches Kazinet station
2. Brothers spot an obstruction on the tracks; Vadim sounds horn and applies emergency brakes; train cannot stop in time
3. Train runs over the obstruction; brothers exit with lanterns; find the remains of seven individuals spread across the tracks — no movement, no signs of life
4. Police dispatched from Abakan station; floodlights brought in; villagers assist in recovery
5. Victims identified as seven 15-year-old boys from Mezhdurechensk: Dmitri, Nikolai, Vadim, Oleg, Vyacheslav, Roman, and Viktor — a close-knit group who had gone into the Siberian taiga August 12 to collect cedar cones
6. Backpacks found approximately 250 meters (820 ft) from the bodies, arranged in a row near the tracks

Phase 2 — Original Investigation and Closure (1989):
7. Officials declare the deaths a tragic accident: the boys allegedly lay down to rest on the tracks after missing their 10:39 p.m. train home; backpack arrangement interpreted by media as possible ritual or group pact
8. Toxicology: no alcohol found in any of the seven victims' systems; all described as healthy, well-fed, experienced in the outdoors
9. Supernenko brothers' testimony: the seven were not standing or walking — they were lying still on the rails as if placed there
10. Parents reject the accident narrative as implausible; no inquiry into why none of the boys moved or woke as the train approached
11. Case officially closed

Phase 3 — Family Campaign and Reopening (1989–1991):
12. For two years, families petition local authorities to reopen the case; phone calls ignored, paperwork lost, doors closed
13. Fall 1991 — families write directly to Russian President Boris Yeltsin; letter reaches the right hands despite Soviet Union collapse
14. October 8, 1991 — Prosecutor General's office officially reopens investigation; Moscow investigators Vladimir Gusenov and Yuri Stallarov assigned

Phase 4 — Second Investigation (1991–1992):
15. Investigators arrive; crime scene has been erased by two years of weather; original evidence mishandled or ignored
16. Vladimir conducts door-to-door interviews in Kazinet; encounters wall of silence from villagers — all claim to have been asleep at the time
17. Repeated mention of local man "Vasili" — currently in prison for killing his brother Slava; Slava allegedly let slip the truth about the seven while drunk before his death
18. Vladimir visits prison; over multiple sessions extracts a partial, reluctant confession from Vasili; Vasili implicates himself and his brother Yuri; then names their dead brother Slava as the primary attacker
19. Vladimir requests and receives permission from families to exhume the bodies
20. Second forensic examination: victims had not been killed by the train; each showed consistent blunt-force injuries — skull fractures, bilateral jaw fractures, chest and abdominal trauma; wound patterns matched tools found in railway depots
21. Biomechanical testing using mannequins confirms railway depot tool matches
22. Supernenko brothers' testimony added: the seven were arranged in a line across the tracks — deliberately placed to ensure train would render the scene unrecognizable

Phase 5 — Full Confession and Murder Reconstruction:
23. A fourth name, Nikolai, surfaces — a police lieutenant visiting Kazinet relatives that weekend; Nikolai claims he left the village before the incident; local witnesses contradict this, placing him drinking with the brothers and the foreman that night
24. A fifth name, Pedor (foreman's brother), is identified but found dead — apparent suicide shortly after investigation reopened
25. Foreman eventually breaks under sustained questioning; provides full account

**Reconstruction of Events (August 12–13, 1989):**
- Evening: Nikolai (police lieutenant), the foreman, his brother Pedor, Vasili, and Yuri drinking together at a house near the tracks; consuming at least 3 liters of homebrew
- Ran out of alcohol; went to the train station to find cargo handlers who might barter for more; found none
- Found the seven 15-year-old boys at the station waiting for the 10:39 p.m. train home
- Claimed the boys were short-circuiting the rails with wire to stop the train; no wire or supporting evidence ever found; boys had no reason to interfere — they knew the schedule
- Confrontation escalated; Nikolai (muscular, armed, in position of authority) struck one of the boys in the head with the butt of his firearm — described as instantly fatal
- Nikolai ordered the other men to kill the remaining boys and leave no witnesses
- Some boys attempted to flee; Nikolai threatened use of his firearm to halt them
- All seven beaten with available tools until dead
- Nikolai directed staging: bodies arranged across the tracks; backpacks lined up approximately 250 meters away to support the story that tired boys lay down to rest
- Men dispersed; 1:40 a.m. train obliterates the positioned remains

Phase 6 — Trial and First Verdicts (1995):
26. Trial convenes 1995, approximately six years after the killings; proceeds for over seven months; courtroom packed with victim families, reporters, and public
27. Verdict: Nikolai, the foreman, Vasili, and Yuri all sentenced to capital punishment
28. Verdict delivery takes over one hour to read aloud

Phase 7 — Collapse of First Verdict (1996):
29. Russia aligns laws with Council of Europe standards; capital punishment abolished
30. May 31, 1996 — First Deputy Chairman of Supreme Court of Russia files protest requesting the verdict be overturned and investigation restarted
31. Supreme Court agrees; sentences overturned; convicted men released without retrial or continued custody

Phase 8 — Aftermath and Post-Release Violence:
32. After release, Vasili attacks a cousin who reportedly knew the truth and was not planning to stay silent during the reopened investigation
33. Yuri (one of the convicted brothers) found dead — cause listed as either suffocation or sudden pneumonia depending on source
34. A railroad worker who helped remove the seven from the tracks in 1989 found dead under a train at Kazinet — officially attributed to slipping and falling

Phase 9 — Third Investigation and Final Verdict (1997–2001):
35. February 1997 — families push Prosecutor General's office to reopen case again under "circumstances not fully investigated"
36. Four additional years of work: three exhumations, five expert forensic reviews, 100+ witnesses, prior testimonies reexamined, mountains of paperwork reviewed
37. During this period, Nikolai — one of the convicted men — had become a municipal deputy (elected official)
38. Fall 2001 — Supreme Court of the Republic of Khakassia issues new verdict: Nikolai, the foreman, and Vasili found guilty of murder; sentences final
39. Final sentences: Nikolai and foreman — 10 years in a maximum security penal colony; Vasili — 7 years
40. Families and public widely characterize the sentences as inadequate given the severity and deliberateness of the killings
41. Nikolai continues filing appeals after sentencing, maintaining he was the real victim

**Observable Mechanics:**
- Seven fit, healthy 15-year-olds subdued by two or three men: possible due to Nikolai's use of firearm as deterrent — halted flight attempts and froze remaining victims
- Backpack staging: line of bags placed 250 meters from bodies — a deliberate forensic counter-narrative, not a coincidence of placement
- Railway tool wound patterns linking killers to occupational access — provided physical corroboration of confessions
- Village wall of silence: small interrelated community with multiple social and familial ties to perpetrators
- Vasili killed his brother Slava (who had knowledge of the killings) — effectively eliminated the most talkative witness before investigators arrived
- A key railroad witness found dead under a train at the same location is the third suspicious post-investigation death in the case
- Nikolai's election to municipal office after first release illustrates the institutional context that allowed the original coverup
