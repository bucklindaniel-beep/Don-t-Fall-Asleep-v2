You don’t need to be inherently creative to come up with creative ideas—you just need one simple trick. Most people think creativity requires some kind of magical talent, but in reality, great ideas come from a straightforward process: combining old ideas in new ways.

Consider this: Steve Jobs didn’t invent the touchscreen, the phone, or the internet—he combined them into the iPhone. Shakespeare borrowed plots from existing stories and improved them. Even Einstein built on prior physics theories to develop relativity. Creativity, at its core, is recombination.

So how can you apply this? The answer is a method called idea stacking.

Step one: absorb relentlessly. Read, watch, and listen widely. Fill your mind with diverse knowledge. The more raw material you have, the easier it becomes to connect ideas. However, don’t fall into the trap of only consuming—start creating early, even if your output is simple. Without action, absorption is useless.

Step two: capture interesting ideas. Write them down, regardless of whether they seem connected. It could be a marketing concept, a scientific fact, or a personal insight—log everything.

Step three: look for unexpected connections. Ask yourself: how can I combine these ideas? What happens if I apply this concept to a different problem? This is where innovation emerges.

For example, James Dyson combined the concept of a vacuum cleaner with the mechanics of industrial cyclones to invent the first bagless vacuum.

The pattern is clear: creativity isn’t about pure originality—it’s about recombination. The best part is that you don’t need to wait for inspiration. Just keep stacking ideas and watch for overlaps.

You can try this immediately. For instance, most podcasts are recorded indoors, while vlogs are often filmed outside. What if you combined the two—recording a podcast in a forest or among ruins? Whether or not it’s entirely new, the point remains: you don’t need talent, just a system that consistently produces results.

Summary:

Creativity is not about innate talent but about combining existing ideas in new ways. By studying examples like Steve Jobs, Shakespeare, and Einstein, we see that innovation often builds on what already exists. The key method—idea stacking—consists of absorbing diverse knowledge, capturing interesting ideas, and actively seeking unexpected connections between them. This process enables consistent creative output without relying on sudden inspiration. Ultimately, creativity becomes a repeatable system rather than a rare gift.
