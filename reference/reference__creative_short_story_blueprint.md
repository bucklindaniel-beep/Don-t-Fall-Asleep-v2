## Show, Don’t Tell: How to Create Immersive, Evidence-Driven Writing

---

### Clean Summary

This guide explores the principle of “show, don’t tell” as a way to deepen reader immersion. While both showing and telling are necessary tools, effective writing uses concrete details, sensory input, and character-driven perspective to let readers infer meaning rather than being directly told what to feel. The goal is to remove the author as an obvious intermediary and allow the reader to experience the story firsthand through evidence, specificity, and narrative voice.

---

### Key Concepts

* Showing = dramatizing; Telling = summarizing
* Readers engage more when they infer meaning themselves
* Concrete details are stronger than abstract statements
* Sensory detail enhances immersion
* Thoughts and actions reveal emotion better than labels
* Narrative voice shapes how information is presented
* Showing and telling should be balanced intentionally

---

### Expanded Notes

#### The Core Problem with Telling

* Telling states conclusions without proof:

  * “She was scared” vs. evidence of fear
* Lacks emotional impact and visualization
* Prevents readers from experiencing the story

#### Showing as Evidence-Based Writing

* Present details that lead readers to conclusions
* Replace “knowing” verbs with observable behavior
* Example:

  * Instead of “He knew she liked him”
  * Show repeated, meaningful interactions

#### Reader Participation (2 + 2 Principle)

* Give readers pieces, not conclusions
* Let them mentally “solve” the story
* Engagement comes from discovery, not explanation

#### Concrete vs. Abstract Language

* Abstract:

  * Emotions (“happy,” “scared”)
  * Opinions (“beautiful,” “strange”)
* Concrete:

  * Actions, physical reactions, sensory cues
* Rule of thumb:

  * If a camera can’t see it, strengthen it

#### Replace Emotion with Action

* Emotions should be inferred through:

  * Behavior
  * Environment interaction
  * Internal thought
* Example:

  * “He was happy” → smiling, jumping, energetic movement

#### Sensory Detail and Specificity

* Strong writing engages:

  * Sight, sound, smell, taste, touch
* Specific details make scenes vivid and unique
* Avoid generic descriptions; aim for distinct imagery

#### Avoid Over-Reliance on Body Language

* Common gestures (clenched fists, sighs) are predictable
* Don’t explain emotion through physical reaction alone
* Focus on:

  * Thought process
  * Context
  * Decision-making

#### Show Through Thought and Perspective

* Internal thoughts reveal emotional depth
* Real-time thinking feels immediate and immersive
* Strong verbs and sentence structure reinforce tone

#### Dialogue as a Tool for Showing

* Dialogue can reveal:

  * Emotion
  * Personality
  * Conflict
* Avoid:

  * Explaining tone with adverbs (“angrily”)
  * Over-explaining intentions
* Let subtext carry meaning

#### Narrative Voice as a Filter

* World is perceived through the character’s lens
* Same scene changes based on perspective
* Avoid “author intrusion” (info meant only for reader)

#### Avoid Infodumps

* Information should serve the character, not just the reader
* Integrate exposition naturally into the scene

#### Showing vs. Telling Balance

* Telling is useful for:

  * Time skips
  * Summaries
  * Background info
* Showing is best for:

  * Emotions
  * Key moments
  * Sensory experiences
  * Character judgments

---

### Actionable Insights

* Replace emotional labels with observable behavior
* Remove “thought verbs” (knew, felt, realized) where possible
* Add at least one concrete detail per abstract idea
* Ask: “Can this be seen, heard, or felt?”
* Use dialogue to imply emotion instead of stating it
* Filter descriptions through character perspective
* Highlight vague sections in drafts and rewrite with specificity
* Use telling strategically for pacing—not as a default
