## Purpose
This file defines repetition pressure as a reusable horror-writing technique.

Use this file to:
- build tension over time without immediate escalation
- convert a single anomaly into a persistent threat
- create psychological pressure through recurrence
- establish patterns before breaking them

---

## Core Definition
Repetition pressure is a technique where an anomaly, sound, event, or behavior repeats over time, gradually increasing tension through frequency, proximity, or variation.

The repetition itself becomes the source of fear.

---

## Why This Technique Works
Repetition creates expectation.

When something repeats:
- the audience anticipates it happening again
- attention becomes focused
- tension builds between occurrences

As repetition continues:
- the audience looks for change
- any variation becomes meaningful
- pressure increases even without direct escalation

---

## Core Effect on the Audience
- builds sustained tension
- creates anticipation between events
- encourages pattern recognition
- amplifies small changes into major signals
- produces psychological discomfort through inevitability

---

## Structural Logic

1. anomaly occurs once
2. anomaly repeats
3. repetition becomes noticeable
4. variation or escalation is introduced
5. pressure increases
6. pattern is broken or escalated

---

## Canonical Pattern

### Step 1 — First occurrence
Introduce an anomaly (sound, movement, message, absence).

### Step 2 — Second occurrence
Repeat the anomaly to establish it is not random.

### Step 3 — Recognition
The character or audience realizes a pattern is forming.

### Step 4 — Reinforcement
The anomaly continues, often with increasing intensity or reduced interval.

### Step 5 — Variation
A change occurs:
- closer
- louder
- different timing
- altered form

### Step 6 — Escalation or break
The pattern leads to:
- confrontation
- reveal
- rule break

---

## Common Variants

### 1. Fixed Interval Repetition
The anomaly occurs at consistent intervals.

**Function:** Creates predictability and anticipation.

---

### 2. Accelerating Repetition
The anomaly occurs more frequently over time.

**Function:** Increases urgency and pressure.

---

### 3. Proximity Repetition
Each occurrence is physically closer.

**Function:** Simulates approach of threat.

---

### 4. Volume / Intensity Repetition
Each occurrence becomes louder or stronger.

**Function:** Escalates perceived danger.

---

### 5. Patterned Repetition
The anomaly follows a recognizable sequence.

**Function:** Encourages decoding and engagement.

---

### 6. Broken Repetition
A pattern is established, then disrupted.

**Function:** Creates shock and unpredictability.

---

## Best Use Cases

Use repetition pressure when you want to:
- stretch tension across a scene
- make small events feel significant
- build anticipation without immediate action
- support psychological escalation
- reinforce a supernatural rule or behavior

---

## When To Avoid It

Avoid repetition pressure when:
- the scene already lacks movement
- there is no variation between repetitions
- the audience already understands the threat fully
- pacing requires rapid progression instead of buildup

---

## Placement In Story Structure

### Early Story
Introduces subtle anomaly.

### Mid-Story
Establishes pattern and builds pressure.

### Pre-Climax
Increases frequency or variation.

### Climax
Pattern is broken or escalated into direct threat.

---

## Pacing Guidance

### To increase pressure
- shorten time between repetitions
- increase intensity or proximity
- reduce certainty

### To maintain tension
- vary each occurrence slightly
- avoid identical repetition

### To maximize impact
- break the pattern unexpectedly
- escalate beyond expected behavior

---

## Language Guidance

### Effective language traits
- simple and consistent phrasing
- repeated structure with variation
- emphasis on timing and spacing

### Example pattern language
- “It happened again.”
- “This time, it was closer.”
- “It didn’t stop.”

---

## Example Application — Minimal
The first knock came late at night.

A few minutes later, it came again.

The third time, it came from inside the house.

---

## Example Application — Escalated
The first night, he heard footsteps outside his window.

The second night, they stopped at his door.

The third night, they started inside the hallway.

He didn’t sleep after that.

---

## Failure Modes

### 1. No variation
Repetition becomes predictable and boring.

### 2. No escalation
Tension plateaus instead of building.

### 3. Overuse
Too many repeated patterns reduce impact.

### 4. Weak payoff
The buildup leads to an underwhelming outcome.

---

## Reusable Writing Rules
- repeat the anomaly, not the exact wording
- vary each occurrence slightly
- move the anomaly closer over time
- reduce time between repetitions
- break the pattern at the right moment

---

## Compression Summary
Repetition pressure = recurring anomaly + variation + escalation.

---

## Notes
