## Purpose
This file defines effective opening hook patterns for horror storytelling.

Use this file to:
- immediately capture attention
- establish tone and credibility
- prevent early audience drop-off
- set narrative expectations

---

## Core Definition
An opening hook is the first sentence or short sequence that establishes interest and compels the audience to continue.

In horror, the hook should:
- feel grounded or intriguing
- imply something is wrong
- avoid over-explaining

---

## Why This Pattern Works
The opening determines whether the audience commits attention.

Effective hooks:
- create curiosity
- establish realism or tension
- introduce uncertainty
- signal that something meaningful will happen

---

## Core Effect on the Audience
- immediate engagement
- curiosity
- expectation of escalation
- willingness to continue

---

## Structural Logic

1. establish context quickly
2. introduce a subtle irregularity or implication
3. avoid full explanation
4. create a question in the audience’s mind

---

## Common Hook Types

### 1. Grounded Statement Hook
A simple, realistic statement that feels like a true story.

**Example Pattern:**  
“I was staying at my friend’s house when this happened.”

**Function:** Builds credibility and relatability.

---

### 2. Subtle Warning Hook
Implies something went wrong without details.

**Example Pattern:**  
“I didn’t think much of it at first—but that changed.”

**Function:** Creates immediate curiosity.

---

### 3. Time-Based Hook
Uses timing to create tension.

**Example Pattern:**  
“This happened around 2:30 in the morning.”

**Function:** Establishes mood and vulnerability.

---

### 4. Contradiction Hook
Introduces something that doesn’t make sense.

**Example Pattern:**  
“The house was empty—but I heard footsteps upstairs.”

**Function:** Immediately introduces anomaly.

---

### 5. Retrospective Hook
Narrator reflects on past event.

**Example Pattern:**  
“I still don’t know what I saw that night.”

**Function:** Signals unresolved tension.

---

### 6. Instructional / Official Hook (Analog Horror)
Sounds like a message or broadcast.

**Example Pattern:**  
“This message is intended for…”

**Function:** Creates detachment and unease.

---

## Best Use Cases

Use strong opening hooks when:
- writing narration scripts
- creating high-retention content
- starting short stories
- introducing analog horror sequences

---

## When To Avoid

Avoid:
- long introductions
- heavy exposition
- overly dramatic statements
- immediate explanation of the threat

---

## Placement Rules

- hook should occur within the first sentence or two
- context should follow immediately after
- escalation should begin shortly after setup

---

---

## Pacing Guidance

### Strong Hooks
- short
- clear
- specific
- slightly incomplete

### Weak Hooks
- vague
- overly detailed
- slow to introduce tension

---

## Language Guidance

### Effective Traits
- simple language
- natural phrasing
- direct tone

### Avoid
- complex descriptions
- abstract ideas
- unnecessary detail

---

## Example Application — Minimal
I didn’t think anything of it at first.

---

## Example Application — Grounded
I was staying at my friend’s house when this happened.

---

## Example Application — Contradiction
The house was empty—but I heard someone upstairs.

---

## Failure Modes

### 1. No hook
Story starts without tension.

### 2. Over-explanation
Removes curiosity.

### 3. Over-dramatic tone
Reduces realism.

### 4. Slow start
Audience disengages early.

---

## Reusable Writing Rules
- start simple
- imply something is wrong
- avoid explaining too much
- create a question in the audience’s mind
- transition quickly into setup

---

## Compression Summary
Opening hook = immediate context + implied anomaly + unresolved question.
