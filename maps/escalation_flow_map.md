# Map — Escalation Flow Map

## Purpose
This file defines how tension escalates across all dimensions of horror writing.

It unifies:
- emotional escalation (Section 1)
- environmental escalation (Section 2)
- psychological escalation (Section 3)
- supernatural escalation (Section 4)
- physical escalation (Section 5)

This is a **multi-layer escalation model** used for:
- scene construction
- pacing control
- narrative consistency
- prompt design

---

## Core Principle
Tension should not increase randomly.

It should escalate across **multiple layers simultaneously or sequentially**, creating compounding pressure.

---

## Unified Escalation Flow

### Stage 1 — Baseline
- Environment: normal or slightly quiet  
- Emotion: neutral or calm  
- Psychology: stable perception  
- Supernatural: none  
- Physical: no reaction  

---

### Stage 2 — Subtle Disruption
- Environment: silence, dim light, stillness  
- Emotion: unease  
- Psychology: doubt  
- Supernatural: anomaly  
- Physical: tension, slight awareness  

---

### Stage 3 — Pattern Recognition
- Environment: repeated irregularities  
- Emotion: anxiety → apprehension  
- Psychology: suspicion  
- Supernatural: recurrence, pattern  
- Physical: increased focus, subtle reactions  

---

### Stage 4 — Escalation
- Environment: distortion, movement, inconsistency  
- Emotion: dread → fear  
- Psychology: paranoia  
- Supernatural: implied rule  
- Physical: trembling, shallow breathing  

---

### Stage 5 — Threshold Crossing
- Environment: clearly hostile or unnatural  
- Emotion: alarm  
- Psychology: loss of certainty  
- Supernatural: rule becomes active  
- Physical: flinch, freeze  

---

### Stage 6 — Active Threat
- Environment: reacting or shifting  
- Emotion: panic  
- Psychology: breakdown begins  
- Supernatural: rule violation or expansion  
- Physical: movement, loss of control  

---

### Stage 7 — Contact / Confrontation
- Environment: fully hostile or unstable  
- Emotion: terror  
- Psychology: collapse of perception  
- Supernatural: direct interaction  
- Physical: contact, pressure, restraint  

---

### Stage 8 — Aftermath / Resolution
- Environment: altered or empty  
- Emotion: dread, shock, or hopelessness  
- Psychology: lingering distortion  
- Supernatural: unresolved or persistent  
- Physical: exhaustion, numbness, injury  

---

## Layer Interaction Model

Escalation can occur in different sequences:

### Linear Escalation
All layers rise together gradually.

---

### Staggered Escalation
One layer leads, others follow.

Example:
- environment shifts first
- psychological doubt follows
- emotional escalation occurs later

---

### Delayed Escalation
One layer remains suppressed until late.

Example:
- no physical contact until climax

---

### Sudden Spike
One layer jumps rapidly.

Example:
- immediate physical threat after slow psychological build

---

## Escalation Control Levers

### Increase Tension By:
- removing information (darkness, silence)
- repeating anomalies
- introducing patterns
- delaying confirmation
- breaking established rules

---

### Decrease Tension By:
- providing explanation
- restoring normalcy
- reducing ambiguity
- resolving patterns

---

## Scene Construction Reference

Use escalation stages to structure scenes:

1. establish baseline  
2. introduce disruption  
3. repeat or reinforce anomaly  
4. escalate emotionally  
5. destabilize perception  
6. activate threat  
7. deliver contact or reveal  
8. resolve or leave open

## Notes

- Not all stories require all stages  
- Skipping stages can create shock but reduce depth  
- Strong horror often uses layered escalation rather than a single dimension  
- This map should guide, not restrict, creative decisions
