## Purpose
This framework defines how pacing and tension interact in horror storytelling.

Use this file to:
- control narrative rhythm
- manage buildup and release
- avoid flat or overwhelming pacing
- optimize audience engagement

---

## Core Principle
Tension should not remain constant.

Effective pacing:
- builds tension
- releases tension
- builds again at a higher level

This creates a wave-like structure.

---

## Tension Curve Model

Tension follows a repeating pattern:

baseline → rise → peak → drop → higher rise → peak

---

## Pacing Phases

### Phase 1 — Baseline
- low tension
- stable environment
- normal pacing

**Function:** establish contrast

---

### Phase 2 — Rising Tension
- anomalies appear
- pacing gradually increases

**Function:** build engagement

---

### Phase 3 — Peak
- highest tension
- confrontation or reveal

**Function:** deliver impact

---

### Phase 4 — Release
- tension drops temporarily
- may include false safety

**Function:** prevent fatigue

---

### Phase 5 — Secondary Rise
- tension returns
- escalates beyond previous level

**Function:** maintain progression

---

### Phase 6 — Final Peak
- strongest moment in story

**Function:** climax

---

## Pacing Controls

### Speed Control

#### Slow Pacing
- longer sentences
- more detail
- longer gaps between events

**Use for:**
- buildup
- atmosphere
- suspense

---

#### Fast Pacing
- short sentences
- minimal detail
- rapid events

**Use for:**
- action
- climax
- panic

---

## Tension Control

### Increase Tension By:
- shortening time between events
- increasing proximity
- reducing clarity
- adding variation
- removing safety

---

### Decrease Tension By:
- providing explanation
- slowing pacing
- restoring environment
- introducing false safety

---

## Rhythm Patterns

### Wave Pattern
Repeated cycles of rise and fall.

---

### Stair-Step Pattern
Each rise is higher than the last.

---

### Spike Pattern
Sudden increase followed by rapid drop.

---

## Integration With Techniques

### Delayed Reveal
Controls when information is released.

---

### Repetition Pressure
Maintains tension between peaks.

---

### False Safety
Creates temporary drops.

---

### Environmental Escalation
Supports continuous background tension.

---

## Scene-Level Application

Within a scene:
- start slow
- build gradually
- accelerate toward peak
- release or transition

---

## Story-Level Application

Across a story:
- multiple tension waves
- each peak higher than the last
- final peak is strongest

---

## Language Guidance

### Slow Sections
- descriptive
- longer sentences
- smoother flow

---

### Fast Sections
- short sentences
- direct phrasing
- rapid progression

---

## Example — Minimal
At first, nothing happened.

Then something moved.

Then it stopped again.

A few seconds later, it happened all at once.

---

## Example — Structured
The house was quiet.

Too quiet.

A sound came from upstairs.

Then nothing.

Then again—closer this time.

By the time he reached the door, it was already open.

---

## Failure Modes

### 1. Flat pacing
No variation in tension.

---

### 2. Constant high tension
Leads to fatigue.

---

### 3. Slow pacing throughout
Loses engagement.

---

### 4. Poor timing of peaks
Climax feels weak or misplaced.

---

## Reusable Rules
- vary pacing intentionally  
- alternate between buildup and release  
- increase intensity over time  
- use shorter sentences for urgency  
- control timing of reveals  

---

## Compression Summary
Pacing system = tension rises and falls in controlled waves.
