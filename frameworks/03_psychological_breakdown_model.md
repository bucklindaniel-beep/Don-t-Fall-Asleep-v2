## Purpose
This framework defines how a character’s psychological state degrades or shifts during a horror narrative.

Use this file to:
- structure internal tension
- create unreliable perception
- increase ambiguity between real and imagined threats
- escalate fear without requiring constant external action

---

## Core Principle
Fear is not only external—it is internal.

Psychological escalation should:
- begin with certainty
- introduce doubt
- destabilize perception
- erode trust in reality
- lead to confusion or collapse

---

## Psychological Layers

1. Perception (what is seen/heard)
2. Interpretation (what it means)
3. Certainty (confidence in reality)
4. Control (ability to respond logically)
5. Identity (sense of self and reality)

---

## Breakdown Stages

### Stage 1 — Stability
- clear perception
- logical thinking
- confidence in reality

**Function:** Establish baseline

---

### Stage 2 — Doubt
- small inconsistencies noticed
- uncertainty begins

**Function:** Introduce instability

---

### Stage 3 — Suspicion
- repeated irregularities
- character begins questioning reality

**Function:** Build tension

---

### Stage 4 — Paranoia
- heightened awareness
- expectation of threat
- misinterpretation possible

**Function:** Intensify internal pressure

---

### Stage 5 — Distortion
- perception becomes unreliable
- conflicting signals
- difficulty distinguishing real vs imagined

**Function:** Break cognitive stability

---

### Stage 6 — Breakdown
- loss of rational processing
- panic or confusion
- inability to trust senses

**Function:** Peak psychological tension

---

### Stage 7 — Collapse or Acceptance
- character either:
  - loses control completely
  - accepts new reality
  - stops questioning

**Function:** Transition to climax or aftermath

---

## Breakdown Patterns

### Gradual Degradation
Slow shift from stability to distortion.

---

### Sudden Shift
Rapid transition from stability to breakdown.

---

### Cyclical Instability
Periods of clarity followed by confusion.

---

### False Recovery
Character believes they are stable again, but are not.

---

## Interaction With External Systems

### Emotional Escalation
Psychological breakdown drives:
- anxiety
- dread
- panic

---

### Environmental Escalation
Environment reinforces:
- confusion
- instability

---

### Supernatural Behavior
May:
- mimic psychological symptoms
- blur line between real and unreal

---

### Physical Escalation
Psychological breakdown affects:
- reaction time
- decision-making
- movement

---

## Ambiguity Control

### Maintain Ambiguity By:
- limiting clear evidence
- allowing multiple interpretations
- delaying confirmation

### Reduce Ambiguity By:
- confirming threat
- showing objective reality

---

## Pacing Guidelines

### Early Stages
- subtle doubt
- minor inconsistencies

### Mid Stages
- repeated anomalies
- increased suspicion

### Late Stages
- rapid distortion
- loss of control

---

## Language Guidance

### Early
- clear, rational language

### Mid
- uncertain phrasing
- questioning tone

### Late
- fragmented sentences
- incomplete thoughts
- urgency

---

## Example Application — Minimal
At first, I thought I was just tired.

But then it happened again.

And this time, I wasn’t sure what I was seeing.

---

## Example Application — Escalated
I kept telling myself it wasn’t real.

But every time I looked away and back again, it was closer.

At some point, I stopped trying to explain it.

---

## Failure Modes

### 1. No progression
Character remains stable despite events.

### 2. Immediate breakdown
No buildup or transition.

### 3. Over-confusion
Audience cannot follow narrative.

### 4. Lack of external anchor
No connection to reality.

---

## Reusable Rules
- start with certainty  
- introduce doubt gradually  
- reinforce inconsistencies  
- escalate to paranoia  
- distort perception  
- break or transform reality  

---

## Compression Summary
Psychological breakdown = stable perception → doubt → paranoia → distortion → collapse.
