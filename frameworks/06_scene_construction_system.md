## Purpose
This framework defines how to construct a complete horror scene.

Use this file to:
- build scenes step-by-step
- maintain tension progression
- integrate multiple escalation systems
- avoid flat or inconsistent scenes

---

## Core Principle
A scene must evolve.

It should:
- start stable
- introduce tension
- escalate consistently
- lead to a clear outcome

---

## Scene Structure Overview

1. Baseline  
2. Trigger  
3. Development  
4. Escalation  
5. Peak  
6. Outcome  

---

## Stage Mapping (System Alignment)

Scene stages MUST map to the escalation model:

- Baseline → Setup  
- Trigger → Disruption  
- Development → Reinforcement  
- Escalation → Escalation  
- Peak → Climax  
- Outcome → Resolution  

The escalation model (/frameworks/01_horror_escalation_model.md) is the authoritative structure.

This scene system defines HOW those stages are executed.

---

## Scene Stages

### Stage 1 — Baseline
- establish setting
- establish character state
- establish narrator voice and audience connection
- environment is stable  

**Function:** provide contrast for later escalation  

**Engagement Rule:** Setup must establish character personality and give the audience a reason to care before the first disruption. Baseline does not mean inert. The narrator's voice, routine, and relationship to their environment must come through before any anomaly appears. A setup that reads like a room inventory without personality is a failure.  

---

### Stage 2 — Trigger
- introduce anomaly or disruption
- something changes  

**Function:** begin tension  

---

### Stage 3 — Development
- reinforce anomaly
- character reacts or investigates  

**Function:** build awareness  

---

### Stage 4 — Escalation
- increase intensity
- combine multiple escalation systems  

**Function:** raise tension  

---

### Stage 5 — Peak
- highest tension point
- confrontation or reveal  

**Function:** deliver impact  

---

### Stage 6 — Outcome
- resolution or continuation  
- may be:
  - escape
  - failure
  - ambiguity  

**Function:** close or transition scene  

---

## System Integration

Each scene should use multiple systems:

### Emotional
- unease → dread → fear → panic  

### Environmental
- stable → irregular → distorted  

### Psychological
- certainty → doubt → paranoia  

### Physical
- no movement → proximity → contact  

---

## Scene Flow Example

1. character enters normal environment  
2. something feels slightly off  
3. anomaly repeats  
4. environment changes  
5. threat becomes clear  
6. confrontation occurs  
7. scene resolves or cuts

### Spike Scene

## Pacing Guidelines

### Early Scene
- slower pacing  
- subtle detail  

### Mid Scene
- increased frequency of events  

### Late Scene
- rapid escalation  
- minimal delay  

---

## Language Guidance

### Early
- descriptive but controlled  

### Mid
- more focused on changes  

### Late
- short, direct sentences  

---

## Scene Boundaries

A scene should end when:
- tension resolves  
- a new phase begins  
- escalation reaches a peak

### 1. No clear structure
Scene feels disorganized  

### 2. No escalation
Scene feels flat  

### 3. Over-escalation

No impact or transition

## Notes
This framework is central to execution.

All horror scenes should follow some variation of this structure.

---

## Pattern-Driven Scene Construction

Each scene MUST:

- reflect its assigned escalation stage  
- apply at least one active pattern from the Pattern & Rule System (/frameworks/07_pattern_and_rule_system.md)  
- introduce new information, pressure, or constraint  

Patterns must be expressed through:

- observable behavior  
- environmental change  
- narrator limitation  
- spatial repositioning  
- interaction shift  

Patterns MUST NOT appear only as abstract concepts.

---

## Scene Progression Rule

All scenes MUST follow the escalation structure defined in:

/frameworks/01_horror_escalation_model.md  

Required sequence:

1. Setup  
2. Disruption  
3. Reinforcement  
4. Escalation  
5. Climax  
6. Resolution  

Each stage must remain structurally distinct.

---

## Escalation Per Scene

Each new scene MUST increase at least one of:

- clarity  
- proximity  
- constraint  
- consequence  

If none of these increase, the scene is not progressing.

---

## Scene Construction Constraints

Scenes MUST NOT:

- repeat the same function as the previous scene  
- repeat the same information without change  
- merge multiple escalation stages unintentionally  
- introduce threat without prior buildup  
- resolve tension before climax  

---

## Scene Validation Rule

A scene FAILS if:

- it does not change the story state  
- it does not advance escalation  
- it duplicates the previous scene’s purpose  
- it introduces escalation without reinforcement  

---

## Sequence Integrity Rule

Across scenes:

- escalation stages MUST progress forward  
- scenes MUST NOT revert to earlier escalation states  
- reinforcement MUST occur before escalation  
- escalation MUST occur before climax  

A sequence FAILS if:

- escalation jumps stages  
- stages are skipped  
- progression resets without narrative cause
